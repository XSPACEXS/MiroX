# Project Overview & Launch GTM — Design Philosophy

## The Core Problem

Launching a product, feature, or go-to-market initiative is one of the most cross-functional activities an organization undertakes. It requires coordination across engineering, design, marketing, sales, customer success, and leadership — each team with its own tools, timelines, terminology, and priorities. The information needed to launch successfully is scattered across Jira tickets, Google Docs, Figma files, Salesforce dashboards, Slack threads, and meeting notes. No single person holds the complete picture.

The deeper problem is fragmentation of understanding. The engineering lead knows the feature status but not the marketing timeline. The marketing manager knows the campaign plan but not the customer research driving it. The sales team knows the competitive landscape but not the design decisions. The PM knows the customer insights but struggles to translate them into aligned execution across all teams.

Traditional project management tools (Jira, Asana, Monday.com) track tasks but not the strategic context behind those tasks. They answer "what is the status?" but not "why are we doing this?", "what did we learn from customers?", "how does this fit the competitive landscape?", or "what does the customer journey look like end-to-end?"

A god-level Project Overview / Launch GTM board solves this by creating a single visual command center that connects the WHY (insights and research), the WHAT (customer journey and experience mapping), the HOW (task execution and tracking), and the WHAT IT LOOKS LIKE (design and creative review) on a single canvas.

## Why Miro for Launch GTM

### What Miro Does That Other Tools Cannot

1. **Cross-functional synthesis**: A Miro board can combine a customer journey map, a kanban task board, research insights, and design mockups on a single canvas. No project management tool, design tool, or analytics platform can do this. The spatial arrangement creates connections that sequential documents cannot.

2. **Strategic context alongside execution**: A Jira board tracks tickets. A Miro Launch GTM board shows WHY those tickets exist (customer insight), WHERE they fit (journey map), WHAT they look like (design review), and HOW they are progressing (task kanban). This transforms task tracking from a mechanical activity into a strategic one.

3. **Visual alignment**: When a VP walks into a launch readiness meeting, a god-level Launch GTM board lets them see the entire initiative in one glance — from customer research to final design to execution status. This reduces meeting time, eliminates "what is the status?" questions, and creates shared understanding.

4. **Flexible structure**: Launches are messy. Requirements change. Timelines shift. Customer feedback alters priorities. A Miro board can be rearranged, annotated, and updated in real time during meetings. The board is a living artifact, not a static report.

5. **Presentation-ready by default**: With proper frame configuration, the same board used for daily work becomes the presentation for the executive launch review. No slide deck creation needed — the board IS the presentation.

### What Makes a God-Level Launch GTM Board

A mediocre Launch GTM board is a collection of sticky notes grouped by team. It looks like a workshop artifact, not a strategic tool. A god-level Launch GTM board does the following:

**It connects insights to execution.** The research findings drive the customer journey map. The journey map reveals the experience gaps. The gaps generate the tasks. The tasks produce the deliverables. This causal chain is visible and traceable.

**It shows the full picture on one canvas.** Not just tasks (Jira does that). Not just designs (Figma does that). Not just research (Dovetail does that). ALL of them, connected, on one surface. The synthesis is the value.

**It serves multiple audiences without multiple documents.** The engineering team focuses on the Tasks quadrant. The design team focuses on the Design quadrant. The strategy team focuses on the Insights quadrant. The customer-facing teams focus on the Mapping quadrant. But they can ALL see how their work connects to everyone else's.

**It tracks readiness, not just status.** Status is "Task X is in progress." Readiness is "Are we ready to launch? Engineering: YES. Marketing: BLOCKED (waiting on legal review). Sales: YES. Support: NO (training not complete)." A god-level board shows launch readiness as a cross-functional assessment.

**It preserves the learning journey.** After the launch, the board becomes a retrospective artifact. What did we learn? What worked? What would we change? The insights, decisions, and outcomes are documented on the same canvas where the work happened.

## Cognitive Principles for Launch GTM Boards

### 1. The Four-Quadrant Principle

The Launch GTM board is organized around four complementary cognitive modes:

- **Insights** (top-left): ANALYSIS — What do we know? What does the data say? What did customers tell us?
- **Mapping** (top-right): SYNTHESIS — How do customers experience the current/future state? What is the journey end-to-end?
- **Tasks** (bottom-left): EXECUTION — What needs to be done? Who is doing it? What is the status?
- **Design** (bottom-right): CREATION — What does it look like? Does the design match the insights and journey?

This arrangement is not arbitrary. It follows the think-plan-do-review cycle that mirrors how cross-functional teams naturally work:

1. Understand (Insights)
2. Map (Journey)
3. Execute (Tasks)
4. Review (Design)

The top half is STRATEGIC (understanding and planning). The bottom half is TACTICAL (execution and deliverables). The left half is ANALYTICAL (data-driven). The right half is EXPERIENTIAL (human-centered). This creates a natural cognitive flow.

### 2. The Single Source of Truth Principle

A launch involves dozens of documents, tools, and communication channels. The Launch GTM board is not a replacement for all of these — it is the integration layer. It links to Jira for detailed tickets, to Figma for full designs, to the research repository for raw data. But it provides the synthesized view that no individual tool can.

**Application**: Every major element on the board should cite its source or link to the detailed version. The board shows the summary; the linked tool shows the detail.

### 3. The Cross-Functional Empathy Principle

Each team sees the launch through their own lens. Engineering sees feature readiness. Marketing sees campaign readiness. Sales sees enablement readiness. A god-level board is designed with empathy for all of these perspectives — it does not privilege one team's view over another.

**Application**: Give each functional area roughly equal visual weight. Do not make the engineering task board 80% of the board with marketing as an afterthought. Balance the quadrants.

### 4. The Readiness Over Status Principle

"Feature X is 80% complete" is status. "We are ready to launch on March 15" is readiness. Status is team-level. Readiness is initiative-level. The board should answer the readiness question, not just aggregate status.

**Application**: Include a prominent "Launch Readiness" section that synthesizes status across all teams into a go/no-go assessment. Use RAG indicators (Red/Amber/Green) for each functional area.

### 5. The Progressive Disclosure Principle

The board has multiple audiences with different depth needs:

| Audience    | Needs                         | Board Interaction            |
| ----------- | ----------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Executive   | Go/no-go, risks, timeline     | 25% zoom, header + readiness |
| PM / Lead   | Cross-functional coordination | 50% zoom, all quadrants      |
| Team Member | Their tasks and context       | 75% zoom, their quadrant     |
| Contributor | Specific card or element      | 100% zoom, single element    |

Design the board so each zoom level serves its audience.

### 6. The Launch Cadence Principle

Launches have natural phases: Research → Planning → Build → Test → Launch → Retrospective. The board evolves through these phases. In Research, the Insights quadrant is active. In Build, the Tasks quadrant dominates. In Launch, the Readiness section is the focus. In Retrospective, the full board becomes a learning artifact.

**Application**: Design the board to support this evolution. Use frame reordering in presentation mode to shift the narrative focus as the launch progresses.

### 7. The Dependency Visibility Principle

The biggest risk in cross-functional launches is unacknowledged dependencies. Marketing cannot launch the campaign until engineering deploys the feature. Sales cannot demo until design delivers the screenshots. Support cannot train until PM finalizes the documentation. A god-level board makes these dependencies explicit and visible.

**Application**: Use connectors between quadrants to show cross-functional dependencies. Add a dedicated "Dependencies" section or overlay that maps critical-path relationships.

## Common Pitfalls in Launch GTM Board Design

### Pitfall 1: The Task-Only Board

A board that is just a kanban task tracker with no strategic context. It shows what is being done but not why, for whom, or how it fits together.
**Fix**: Add the Insights and Mapping quadrants. Connect tasks to the customer insights that generated them.

### Pitfall 2: The Beautiful But Static Dashboard

A polished board created at the beginning of a project that nobody updates because it is too complex to maintain.
**Fix**: Design for maintainability. Use sticky notes for things that change frequently. Keep the structure simple enough that any team member can update their section in under 5 minutes.

### Pitfall 3: The Team Silo Board

A board with separate sections for each team that do not connect to each other. Engineering, marketing, and sales each have their area, but there are no cross-references, dependencies, or shared timeline.
**Fix**: Add explicit cross-functional connections. Include a shared milestone timeline. Use the Mapping quadrant as the connective tissue that shows how each team's work contributes to the customer experience.

### Pitfall 4: The Information Dump

A board that tries to include everything — every research finding, every design iteration, every task, every meeting note. The board becomes a warehouse, not a workspace.
**Fix**: Apply the progressive disclosure principle. The board shows summaries with links to details. Include 3-5 key insights, not 50. Show the current design, not every iteration. Display active tasks, not completed ones.

### Pitfall 5: No Launch Readiness View

A board that tracks individual team progress but never answers the question "Are we ready to launch?" with a cross-functional assessment.
**Fix**: Add a "Launch Readiness" frame with RAG indicators per team and an overall go/no-go assessment. This should be the first frame in presentation mode.

## The God-Level Standard for Launch GTM Boards

1. **Four-quadrant structure**: Insights, Mapping, Tasks, and Design are all represented with balanced visual weight
2. **Insight-to-execution traceability**: Tasks can be traced back to the customer insights that generated them
3. **Cross-functional empathy**: Every team can find their work and understand how it connects to others
4. **Launch readiness view**: A single, prominent assessment of cross-functional launch preparedness
5. **Dependency visibility**: Cross-team dependencies are explicitly mapped and monitored
6. **Progressive disclosure**: Serves executives at 25% zoom and contributors at 100% zoom
7. **Presentation-ready**: The board can be presented in a launch review meeting without additional preparation
8. **Maintainable**: Any team member can update their section in under 5 minutes
9. **Phase-adaptive**: The board evolves naturally from research through planning through execution through retrospective
10. **Source-linked**: Every insight, design, and data point links back to its authoritative source
