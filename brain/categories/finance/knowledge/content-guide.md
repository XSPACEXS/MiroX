# Content Guide — Finance Boards

## Overview

Financial content must be simultaneously precise and comprehensible. The CFO needs exact numbers. The CEO needs the story. The board of directors needs the implications. This guide covers the domain knowledge, essential sections, real content examples, financial copywriting principles, and data visualization approaches that make finance boards decision-ready.

---

## Essential Sections by Board Type

### Budget Planning Board — Required Sections

| Section              | Purpose                                  | Content Type                                 |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Budget Summary       | Headline numbers for the total budget    | Total, YoY change, headcount, key ratios     |
| Department Breakdown | How the budget is allocated across teams | Department table with budget, change, status |
| Allocation Visual    | Proportional comparison of departments   | Pie chart, treemap, or bar chart             |
| Variance Tracking    | Actual vs. budget performance            | Variance table by department and category    |
| Cash Flow Forecast   | Monthly inflows and outflows             | 12-month waterfall or bar chart              |
| Assumptions & Risks  | What the budget depends on               | Explicit assumptions with sensitivity notes  |
| Approval Workflow    | Status of budget approvals               | Stage tracker: Draft → Review → Approved     |

### Financial Dashboard — Required Sections

| Section           | Purpose                             | Content Type                                   |
| ----------------- | ----------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| KPI Scorecard     | Top-line financial health           | 4-6 KPI cards with values, comparisons, health |
| Revenue Trend     | How revenue is tracking over time   | 12-month line chart with target overlay        |
| Expense Breakdown | Where money is being spent          | Category breakdown (dept, type, or project)    |
| P&L Summary       | Profit and loss overview            | Condensed income statement table               |
| Cash Position     | Current cash and runway             | Cash balance, monthly burn, runway months      |
| Key Alerts        | Items requiring immediate attention | Prioritized alert list with owners             |

### Investment Analysis — Required Sections

| Section               | Purpose                   | Content Type                                 |
| --------------------- | ------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Investment Thesis     | Context for the analysis  | Strategic rationale, decision timeline       |
| Comparison Matrix     | Side-by-side evaluation   | Multi-criteria scoring grid                  |
| Financial Projections | Forward-looking numbers   | Revenue, costs, ROI, IRR per option          |
| Risk Assessment       | What could go wrong       | Risk-return matrix per option                |
| Due Diligence Status  | What has been verified    | Checklist per option                         |
| Recommendation        | Conclusion and next steps | Weighted score, recommended action, timeline |

### Revenue Model Canvas — Required Sections

| Section             | Purpose                          | Content Type                            |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Revenue Streams     | Where revenue comes from         | Stream breakdown with size and growth   |
| Pricing & Packaging | How the product is priced        | Tier structure, pricing logic           |
| Unit Economics      | Per-customer profitability       | CAC, LTV, payback, gross margin         |
| Cohort Analysis     | Retention and expansion patterns | Monthly cohort grid, retention curves   |
| Growth Projections  | Future revenue scenarios         | Base, optimistic, pessimistic forecasts |
| Key Assumptions     | What drives the projections      | Assumption list with sensitivity        |

### Example 4: Revenue Model — Unit Economics

```
Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC):
  Sales & Marketing Spend (Q4): $1,800,000
  New Customers Acquired (Q4): 4,000
  CAC = $450

Customer Lifetime Value (LTV):
  Average Revenue Per Account (ARPA): $588/year
  Gross Margin: 78%
  Churn Rate: 8% annual
  LTV = ($588 x 0.78) / 0.08 = $5,733

LTV:CAC Ratio: $5,733 / $450 = 12.7x
  (Target: >3.0x — EXCELLENT)

Payback Period:
  CAC / (ARPA x Gross Margin / 12) = $450 / $38.22 = 11.8 months
  (Target: <18 months — GOOD)
```

## Financial Copywriting Principles

### 1. Lead with the Variance, Not the Actual

**Bad**: "Engineering spent $10,200,000 in FY2025."
**Good**: "Engineering is $400,000 (4.1%) over budget — driven by 3 unplanned senior hires to address platform stability issues."

The actual is context. The variance is the insight. Always lead with what has changed or deviated.

### 2. Pair Every Number with a "So What?"

**Bad**: "Gross margin: 62.5%"
**Good**: "Gross margin: 62.5% (target: 64.0%). The 1.5pp compression is driven by increased cloud hosting costs from the Southeast Asia expansion. If not addressed, projects to $2.1M annual margin erosion."

Numbers without interpretation are data. Numbers with interpretation are intelligence.

### 3. Use Concrete Time References

**Bad**: "Revenue is growing."
**Good**: "Revenue grew 8.3% YoY in FY2025 (vs. 12.1% in FY2024). Growth deceleration is primarily attributable to mid-market churn in Q3."

Always specify: which period, compared to what, and why the change.

### 4. State Assumptions Explicitly

**Bad**: "We project $42M in revenue for FY2026."
**Good**: "We project $42M in revenue for FY2026, assuming: (1) 15% QoQ growth (current trend: 12%), (2) enterprise deal closure rate stays above 35%, (3) no major competitor pricing disruption. If assumption #1 drops to 10%, projection falls to $38M."

Projections without assumptions are opinions. Projections with assumptions are models.

### 5. Write Alerts as Action Items

**Bad**: "Sales & Marketing is 21% over budget."
**Good**: "ALERT: S&M 21% over budget ($1.3M overspend). Root cause: unbudgeted agency spend ($800K) for Q4 product launch campaign. Action required: VP Marketing to submit variance justification to CFO by March 15. Reforecasting recommended for Q3-Q4."

Alerts should include: what happened, why, who owns it, and what to do.

## Questions Each Board Must Answer

### Budget Planning Board:

1. What is our total budget and how does it compare to last year?
2. How is the budget allocated across departments?
3. Which departments have the largest increases and why?
4. What headcount is assumed?
5. What are the key budget risks?
6. What assumptions underlie the forecast?
7. Where are we in the approval process?
8. What decisions are still pending?

### Financial Dashboard:

1. How is revenue performing vs. target?
2. Are expenses on track?
3. What is our current margin?
4. How much cash do we have and how long will it last?
5. What trends are emerging?
6. What requires immediate attention?

### Investment Analysis:

1. What are we evaluating and why?
2. How do options compare across key criteria?
3. What is the expected financial return of each option?
4. What are the risks?
5. Which option is recommended and why?
6. What is the decision timeline?

### Revenue Model Canvas:

1. Where does our revenue come from?
2. How is our product priced?
3. What are our unit economics?
4. How well do we retain and expand customers?
5. What does revenue look like in 12-24 months?
6. What are the biggest risks to the projection?

## Data Visualization Best Practices

### Chart Type Selection

| Data Story                   | Best Chart           | Why                                    |
| ---------------------------- | -------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Revenue trend over time      | Line chart           | Shows trajectory and inflection points |
| Budget allocation by dept    | Treemap or pie       | Shows proportional composition         |
| Budget vs. actual variance   | Grouped bar chart    | Enables direct comparison per category |
| Cash flow over time          | Waterfall chart      | Shows how components sum to total      |
| Investment option comparison | Radar chart or table | Multi-criteria comparison              |
| Revenue by stream            | Stacked bar chart    | Shows composition and total            |
| Scenario projections         | Fan chart (3 lines)  | Shows range of possible outcomes       |

### The KPI Pyramid

Structure KPIs in a hierarchy:

**Level 1 — The One Number** (visible at 20% zoom):

- Total revenue, total budget, or EBITDA

**Level 2 — The Supporting 4-6** (visible at 50% zoom):

- Revenue, COGS, Gross Margin, OpEx, Net Income, Cash

**Level 3 — The Detail 12-20** (visible at 100% zoom):

- Department budgets, individual cost categories, sub-metrics

This pyramid ensures the board works at every zoom level.

## Common Content Mistakes

### 1. Showing Only Actuals

A board full of current values with no comparison to budget, prior period, or target tells you where you are but not whether that is good or bad. Always include comparison context.

### 2. Mismatched Periods

Comparing Q4 actuals to full-year budget makes the numbers look artificially low. Ensure apples-to-apples period matching. Label every number with its time period.

### 3. Hidden Assumptions

A revenue projection without stated assumptions is an opinion dressed as data. Always surface the 3-5 key assumptions that the forecast depends on.

### 4. Round Number Syndrome

Presenting "$24M" when the actual is "$24,537,891" erodes trust with finance audiences. Show precise numbers in tables and annotations, even if headline KPIs use rounded values.

### 5. Missing the "What Next?"

Dashboards that monitor without recommending are passive tools. Always include an actions section: what needs to change, who owns it, and by when.

## Content Templates

### KPI Card Template

```
Metric Name: [Revenue / Expenses / EBITDA / Cash]
Current Value: [Precise number with currency]
Comparison: [vs. target, vs. prior period, vs. benchmark]
Variance: [+/- amount and percentage]
Health: [On Track / At Risk / Off Track]
Source: [Data system and date]
```

### Budget Line Item Template

```
Department: [Name]
FY Current Budget: [Amount]
FY Prior Actual: [Amount]
Change: [+/- amount and percentage]
Key Driver: [Why the change]
Status: [Draft / Under Review / Approved]
Owner: [VP or Director name]
```

### Assumption Template

```
Assumption: [Clear statement]
Basis: [Historical trend, market data, or executive estimate]
Sensitivity: [What happens if this assumption is wrong by +/- X%]
Risk Level: [Low / Medium / High]
Last Validated: [Date]
```

### Alert Template

```
Severity: [Critical / Warning / Informational]
What: [What happened — specific metric and deviation]
Why: [Root cause — why did this happen]
Impact: [Financial impact if not addressed]
Action: [What needs to be done]
Owner: [Name and team]
Deadline: [Date]
```
