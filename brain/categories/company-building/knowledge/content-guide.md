# Company Building — Content Guide

## Overview

The content on company building boards is the heart of the artifact. While visual design attracts the eye, it is the content — the vision statement, the value definitions, the hiring plan, the investor narrative — that creates organizational alignment and drives action. This guide covers the domain knowledge, content frameworks, and example content that make company building boards authoritative and useful.

---

## Domain Knowledge: Core Concepts

### Vision, Mission, and Purpose

These three concepts are often confused. Precise definitions:

**Vision**: The future state of the world the company aims to create. It is aspirational, time-horizon-free, and bigger than the company itself.

- Format: 1 sentence, maximum 20 words
- Example: "A world where every person has access to personalized financial education."
- Test: Does this inspire someone who does not work here?

**Mission**: What the company does, for whom, and how. It is actionable, present-tense, and company-specific.

- Format: 2-3 sentences
- Example: "We build AI-powered learning tools that make financial concepts accessible to anyone with a smartphone. We partner with schools, employers, and governments to distribute financial literacy at scale."
- Test: Could a new employee describe what we do after reading this?

**Purpose**: Why the company exists beyond profit. It is the founder's motivation, the emotional driver.

- Format: 1 sentence
- Example: "We exist because financial illiteracy is the invisible tax on the world's most vulnerable populations."
- Test: Does this explain why the founders started this company instead of doing something else?

### Values and Culture

**Values**: The 3-5 non-negotiable principles that guide how the company makes decisions.

**Good values are**:

- Specific to the company (not universal truths like "integrity")
- Behavioral (describe what people DO, not what they believe)
- Sometimes uncomfortable (real values require trade-offs)
- Memorable (short enough to recall without looking them up)

**Value definition framework**:

```
Value Name: [2-4 words]
Statement: [1-2 sentences explaining the value]
We Do:
  - [Specific observable behavior 1]
  - [Specific observable behavior 2]
  - [Specific observable behavior 3]
We Don't:
  - [Specific behavior this value rejects]
  - [Specific behavior this value rejects]
Tension: [What this value costs us / what we sacrifice]
Example: [A real story where this value was tested]
```

**Example value definitions**:

```
SPEED OVER PERFECTION
We ship fast and iterate. A good feature in users' hands today is worth more
than a perfect feature next month. We accept technical debt consciously
and pay it down deliberately.

We Do:
  - Ship MVPs within 2-week cycles
  - Make decisions with 70% information, not 100%
  - Celebrate shipping, not planning

We Don't:
  - Gold-plate features before shipping
  - Block launches for edge case handling
  - Require consensus before acting

Tension: Sometimes we ship bugs. We accept this because speed compounds.
Example: "We launched v2 with a known billing edge case affecting <1% of users.
We shipped Monday, fixed Wednesday. The 99% had the feature 2 weeks earlier."
```

```
RADICAL TRANSPARENCY
We share information by default, not by request. Financial performance, strategic
decisions, and even failures are visible to the entire team. Trust is built
through openness.

We Do:
  - Share the investor update with the whole company
  - Post-mortem every failure publicly (blameless)
  - Make salary bands visible

We Don't:
  - Hoard information in leadership meetings
  - Sugarcoat financial challenges
  - Surprise the team with strategic pivots

Tension: Sometimes information causes anxiety before context is fully formed.
Example: "When we missed Q3 targets by 20%, we shared the miss, the analysis,
and the recovery plan in the same all-hands. Result: zero attrition."
```

### Anti-Values

Anti-values define what the company explicitly rejects. They are powerful because they draw boundaries:

```
WE DON'T DO:
  - "Move fast and break things" without cleaning up
  - Heroic individual effort over sustainable teamwork
  - Politicized decision-making based on seniority
  - Building in stealth when customers could give feedback
  - Celebrating hours worked instead of outcomes achieved
```

### Strategic Pillars

Strategic pillars are the 3-4 major bets the company is making. Each pillar should be:

**Pillar format**:

```
Pillar Name: [2-4 words]
Description: [2-3 sentences — what this pillar means]
Key Results (OKRs):
  - KR1: [Measurable outcome with target]
  - KR2: [Measurable outcome with target]
  - KR3: [Measurable outcome with target]
Owner: [VP or team lead responsible]
Timeline: [Quarter or date range]
Status: [On Track / At Risk / Behind / Completed]
Dependencies: [What this pillar needs from other pillars]
```

**Example pillar**:

```
PRODUCT-LED GROWTH
Build a product experience so compelling that users become our primary
acquisition channel through referrals, word of mouth, and organic discovery.
Reduce dependence on paid acquisition by 50% within 12 months.

Key Results:
  - 40% of new signups come from referrals (current: 18%)
  - Time-to-value under 5 minutes for new users (current: 12 min)
  - NPS exceeds 60 (current: 42)

Owner: VP Product
Timeline: Q1-Q4 2025
Status: On Track (Q2 check-in: referrals at 24%)
Dependencies: Requires engineering investment in onboarding flow (Pillar 2)
```

## Content Structures by Board Type

### Company Vision Board Content

**Required sections**:

1. **Company Identity Block**:
   - Logo
   - Company name
   - Tagline (if exists)
   - Founded date
   - HQ location
   - Website

2. **Vision Statement**:
   - Display-size text, centered
   - 1 sentence, bold and aspirational

3. **Mission Statement**:
   - 2-3 sentences below the vision
   - What we do, for whom, how

4. **Purpose / Origin Story**:
   - 2-3 sentences on why the company exists
   - Optional: Founder quote

5. **Strategic Pillars** (3-4):
   - Pillar name, description, key results, owner, status
   - Visual status indicator (green/amber/red dot)

6. **Key Metrics Dashboard** (5-8 metrics):
   - ARR/MRR, user count, growth rate, NPS, team size, runway
   - Each with current value, trend, and target

7. **North Star Metric**:
   - The ONE metric that matters most, displayed prominently
   - Example: "Weekly Active Learners: 47,200 (+12% MoM)"

8. **Team Snapshot**:
   - Headcount, department breakdown
   - Key leaders with names and roles

9. **Timeline / Milestones**:
   - Key past achievements
   - Upcoming milestones (next 12 months)

### Culture Canvas Content

**Required sections**:

1. **Culture Identity Header**:
   - "Our Culture" or "How We Work"
   - Company tagline or culture one-liner
   - Last updated date

2. **Values Grid** (3-5 values):
   - Each value: name, statement, behaviors (do/don't), example
   - Equal visual weight per value

3. **Anti-Values**:
   - What we explicitly reject
   - 3-5 anti-values with brief explanation

4. **Decision-Making Principles**:
   - How we make decisions (consensus, disagree-and-commit, owner decides)
   - Escalation path
   - Speed expectations

5. **Communication Norms**:
   - Meeting culture (async-first, agenda-required, max length)
   - Documentation expectations
   - Feedback style (direct, written, real-time)

6. **Rituals and Traditions**:
   - Regular cadence items (Monday standup, Friday demo, monthly retro)
   - Celebrations (shipping bells, milestone parties)
   - Unique traditions (annual hackathon, team book club)

7. **Hiring Values Filter**:
   - "We hire for" list (traits beyond skills)
   - Interview questions that test values alignment
   - Example: "Tell me about a time you shipped something imperfect"

### Org Growth Plan Content

**Required sections**:

1. **Current Org Snapshot**:
   - Total headcount
   - Department breakdown (Engineering, Product, GTM, G&A)
   - Org chart showing reporting structure
   - Key leadership roles (filled and open)

2. **Growth Timeline** (3-5 time periods):
   - For each period: target headcount, key hires, department growth
   - Budget impact (annualized cost per period)

3. **Hiring Priority Stack**:
   - Ranked list of next hires with rationale
   - Each hire: role, department, start date target, justification
   - Example: "#1: Sr. Backend Engineer — needed for v3 platform rewrite"

4. **Team Topology**:
   - How teams are structured (functional, cross-functional, squad model)
   - Planned evolution (e.g., "Move from functional to squad model at 25 people")
   - Team lead identification

5. **Leadership Pipeline**:
   - Current leaders and their span of control
   - Identified future leaders (internal promotion candidates)
   - Leadership hires needed (VP Engineering by Q3, etc.)

6. **Compensation & Budget**:
   - Salary band overview (without individual salaries)
   - Total people cost as % of revenue
   - Equity allocation strategy
   - Benefits highlights

### Investor Update Board Content

**Required sections**:

1. **Header**:
   - Company name and logo
   - Update period (e.g., "Q4 2025")
   - Confidentiality notice

2. **Headline Metrics** (5-8 cards):
   - ARR/MRR, growth rate, net revenue retention
   - User metrics (DAU, MAU, conversion)
   - Financial metrics (burn rate, runway, CAC, LTV)
   - Each metric: current value, MoM change, trend sparkline

3. **Narrative: Wins**:
   - 3-5 key achievements this quarter
   - Concrete, specific, and quantified
   - Example: "Closed 3 enterprise deals totaling $240K ARR"

4. **Narrative: Challenges**:
   - 2-3 honest challenges
   - With root cause and action plan
   - Example: "Churn increased to 5.2% — root cause: onboarding complexity. Fix: launching guided setup flow in Q1."

5. **Product Update**:
   - What shipped this quarter
   - What is planned next quarter
   - Key technical/product decisions

6. **Team Update**:
   - New hires and departures
   - Open roles and hiring status
   - Team morale indicator

7. **Financial Summary**:
   - Revenue, expenses, burn rate, runway
   - Cash position
   - Fundraising status (if applicable)

8. **The Ask**:
   - 2-3 specific, actionable requests for investors
   - Example: "Introductions to VP-level buyers at Fortune 500 retailers"
   - Example: "Advice on structuring an enterprise sales team"

## Metrics Content Deep Dive

### Metric Categories

**Growth Metrics** (how fast are we growing):

- MRR and ARR with MoM/YoY growth rates
- New customers per month
- User signups and activation rate
- Expansion revenue (upsells, cross-sells)

**Retention Metrics** (are we keeping what we win):

- Logo churn rate (% of customers lost)
- Revenue churn rate (% of revenue lost)
- Net Revenue Retention (target: >110% for SaaS)
- Cohort retention curves

**Efficiency Metrics** (are we spending wisely):

- CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
- LTV:CAC ratio (target: >3:1)
- Payback period (months to recover CAC)
- Burn multiple (net burn / net new ARR)

**Product Metrics** (is the product working):

- DAU/MAU ratio (engagement depth)
- Time to value (onboarding speed)
- Feature adoption rates
- NPS or CSAT

**People Metrics** (is the team healthy):

- Headcount and growth rate
- Offer acceptance rate
- Time to hire (days per role)
- Employee NPS / engagement score
- Attrition rate (voluntary/involuntary)

### Metric Presentation Rules

1. **Always show trajectory**: A number without context is meaningless. Show MoM or QoQ change.
2. **Compare to target**: Show progress toward a stated goal.
3. **Contextualize**: "Our NRR is 115% vs. industry median of 105%."
4. **Group logically**: Growth metrics together, retention together, efficiency together.
5. **Lead with North Star**: The most important metric gets the most visual prominence.
6. **Honest with challenges**: If churn is up, show it. Investors respect transparency.

## Investor Communication Best Practices

### The Monthly/Quarterly Update Formula

**Structure** (each section = 2-3 sentences):

1. **TL;DR**: One paragraph summary of the quarter. Lead with the most important news.
2. **Key Metrics**: Dashboard of 5-8 headline numbers.
3. **Highlights**: 3-5 wins, specific and quantified.
4. **Lowlights**: 2-3 challenges, with root cause and plan.
5. **Product**: What shipped, what is next.
6. **Team**: Hires, departures, culture notes.
7. **Financials**: Revenue, burn, runway.
8. **Ask**: 2-3 specific, actionable requests.

### What Investors Want to See

- **Consistency**: Same format every time. They should know where to find MRR.
- **Honesty**: Acknowledge problems. Never hide bad news.
- **Trend**: Quarter-over-quarter progression. Are things getting better?
- **Narrative**: Not just numbers — the story of why numbers changed.
- **Action**: What you are doing about challenges. Proactive, not reactive.
- **Specific asks**: Not "help us grow" but "introduce us to [specific person/company]."

### What Investors Do NOT Want

- Vanity metrics without context (downloads, page views, registered users who never returned)
- Paragraphs of text instead of structured updates
- Only good news (this triggers suspicion, not confidence)
- Changing the format every quarter (makes comparison impossible)
- Vague asks ("We could use some help with sales")

## Content Quality Checklist

Before a board is complete, verify:

- [ ] Vision statement is 1 sentence, under 20 words, and aspirational
- [ ] Mission statement describes what, for whom, and how
- [ ] Each value has a name, statement, and specific behaviors
- [ ] Strategic pillars have key results, owners, and status indicators
- [ ] Key metrics include current value AND trend direction
- [ ] The North Star metric is visually prominent
- [ ] Content is specific to THIS company (not generic)
- [ ] Any aspirational claim has a corresponding accountability metric
- [ ] The board has been updated within the last quarter
- [ ] For investor boards: the ask section has 2-3 specific, actionable requests
- [ ] No sensitive data (individual salaries, personal info) on shared boards
- [ ] Company brand tone matches the content voice

---

## Summary

Content on company building boards must be specific, honest, and actionable. Vision statements inspire but are grounded by metrics. Values are behavioral, not aspirational platitudes. Growth plans are sequenced and justified. Investor updates are structured, transparent, and narrative-driven. Every piece of content earns its place by answering one of the fundamental questions: Who are we? Where are we going? How are we getting there? And how are we doing?
