# Customer Journey Map

## Overview

- **Purpose**: A comprehensive visual map of the entire customer journey from first awareness through long-term advocacy, layering touchpoints, emotional states, pain points, and opportunities at each stage. This is the single most powerful artifact for aligning an organization around customer experience — making the invisible visible and turning empathy into action.
- **Best For**: Product managers, UX researchers, CX teams, and leadership seeking to understand, diagnose, and improve the end-to-end customer experience for a specific persona or segment.
- **Complexity**: Advanced
- **Board Size**: 5000 x 3000px

## Board Layout

The board is organized as a horizontal journey with 6 stages as columns. Rows represent different lenses: actions, touchpoints, thoughts/emotions, pain points, and opportunities. An emotion curve runs through the middle connecting all stages visually. A persona card sits on the left, and a summary/actions frame sits on the right.

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------------+
| [ JOURNEY MAP TITLE ]                                                    |
+------------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

## Connectors & Flow

- Horizontal arrows connecting all 6 stages left to right (the journey flow)
- The Emotion Curve spans all stages as a continuous line with labeled data points
- Vertical dashed lines separating each stage column
- Arrows from Pain Points (red) to corresponding Opportunities (gold) within each stage
- Bold arrow from the "DANGER ZONE" label to Priority Action #1
- Arrow from Key Metrics frame to the Emotion Curve (data informs the journey)

## Example Content

The journey map is fully pre-filled for a B2B SaaS project management tool targeting mid-market operations managers:

- **Persona**: Rachel Torres, 34, Operations Manager, 150-person SaaS company
- **6 stages**: Awareness, Consideration, Purchase, Onboarding, Retention, Advocacy
- **24 customer actions** across all stages with specific, realistic behaviors
- **23 touchpoints** across web, email, product, sales, support, and community channels
- **6 emotion states** plotted on a curve with an identified "danger zone" at onboarding
- **13 pain points** with specific, actionable descriptions

## Variations

1. **B2C E-Commerce Journey**: Replace Purchase stage with Cart/Checkout. Add a Post-Purchase stage (shipping, unboxing). Focus on repeat purchase loop.
2. **Service Journey (Healthcare, Banking)**: Add a "Service Delivery" stage between Purchase and Retention. Include staff interactions as key touchpoints.
3. **Current State vs. Future State**: Create two parallel journey maps — one showing today's experience (red-highlighted pain points) and one showing the designed future state (green-highlighted improvements).
4. **Micro-Journey**: Zoom into a single stage (e.g., just Onboarding) and break it into sub-steps with much more detail per step.

1. Create board at 5000 x 3000px with Cloud White (#F5F5F5) background.

10. Draw horizontal flow arrows between stages and vertical dashed separators.

## Tips & Best Practices

- Base the journey map on real research — interviews, analytics, support tickets, and session recordings. Do not guess.
- One persona per map. If you serve multiple segments, create separate maps for each.
- The emotion curve is the most powerful element. When a stakeholder sees the onboarding dip, they understand the urgency instantly.
- Pain points should be specific enough to act on. "Onboarding is hard" is useless. "Data import from spreadsheets takes 3 hours of manual work" is actionable.
- Review the journey map quarterly and update it with new data. A stale map is worse than no map.
- Present the map to the full company at least once — engineering, sales, support, and leadership should all see the customer's world.
- Use the journey map to prioritize your product roadmap. The biggest pain point at the most critical stage should be your #1 priority.
- Hang a printed version on the office wall (or pin it in Slack) so it becomes part of the team's shared mental model.
