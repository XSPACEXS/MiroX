# Empathy Map

## Overview

- **Purpose**: A classic 4-quadrant empathy map that captures what a specific user persona Says, Thinks, Does, and Feels. This synthesis tool is used after user interviews, observations, or research to distill raw qualitative data into a structured understanding of the user's inner world. The empathy map bridges the gap between data and insight, helping teams internalize the user's perspective before designing solutions.
- **Best For**: UX researchers synthesizing interview findings, product teams building shared empathy after user research sessions, and workshop facilitators guiding teams through persona development exercises.
- **Complexity**: Simple
- **Board Size**: 3000 x 2500px

## Board Layout

The board centers on a persona card in the middle, surrounded by four quadrants: Says (top-left), Thinks (top-right), Does (bottom-left), Feels (bottom-right). A pain/gain summary sits at the bottom.

## Connectors & Flow

- Four dashed lines from the central persona card outward to each quadrant (Says, Thinks, Does, Feels)
- Subtle curved connectors between Says and Thinks (what they say publicly vs. what they think privately — often different)
- Subtle curved connectors between Does and Feels (behaviors driven by emotions)
- Arrows from both Pains and Gains pointing toward the persona (these summarize the quadrants)
- A circular flow suggestion: Says -> Thinks -> Feels -> Does (the insight cycle)

## Variations

1. **Team Synthesis Version**: Run this as a workshop exercise after a round of user interviews. Each team member fills in sticky notes based on their interviews, then the team clusters and discusses patterns.
2. **Comparative Empathy Maps**: Create 2-3 empathy maps side by side for different personas (e.g., first-time buyer vs. downsizer vs. investor) to highlight differences in Says/Thinks/Does/Feels.
3. **Before & After Empathy Map**: Map the user's state before using your product and after — showing how the product changes their emotional landscape.
4. **Minimal Version**: Use just Says and Feels for a quick 15-minute synthesis when time is limited.
5. **Extended Version**: Add a "Hears" section (what influences from media, peers, and ads reach them) and a "Sees" section (what they observe in their environment).

## Tips & Best Practices

- Always base the empathy map on real research, not assumptions. If you are guessing, label it "Hypothesis Empathy Map" and validate with interviews.
- Says vs. Thinks is the most revealing contrast. What people say in public often differs from what they think privately — this gap is where design insights live.
- Use direct quotes in the Says quadrant. "I am frustrated with the process" is weaker than "'I thought I understood mortgages until I actually applied.'"
- Feels should capture the full emotional range — not just negative emotions. Excitement, hope, and pride are as important as anxiety and frustration.
- Does should focus on observable behaviors, not intentions. "Plans to compare tools" is a thought. "Maintains a spreadsheet comparing 28 homes" is a behavior.
- The Pains and Gains summary sections are optional but powerful — they bridge the empathy map to opportunity identification.
- Print the empathy map and post it in the team's workspace. Refer to it by the persona's name: "What would Daniel think about this feature?"
- Update the empathy map after each research round. New interviews should deepen the picture, not just confirm existing assumptions.
