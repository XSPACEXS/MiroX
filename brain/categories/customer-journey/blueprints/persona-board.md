# User Persona Board

## Overview

- **Purpose**: A comprehensive user persona board that goes beyond basic demographics to paint a vivid, research-backed portrait of a target user — including their goals, frustrations, daily behaviors, technology proficiency, a "day in the life" narrative, and representative quotes. This persona becomes the team's shared reference point when making product, design, and marketing decisions.
- **Best For**: Product managers defining target users, UX designers grounding their work in user reality, marketing teams crafting messaging, and leadership teams aligning on who the product serves.
- **Complexity**: Medium
- **Board Size**: 3500 x 2500px

## Board Layout

The board is structured as a large persona profile "poster" with a photo and demographics header on the left, and detailed attribute sections arranged in a magazine-style layout across the remaining space.

## Example Content

The persona board is fully pre-filled for a B2B growth analytics platform targeting startup founders:

- **Persona**: Mia Johannsen, 36, CEO & Co-founder of a Series A SaaS startup (45 employees)
- **5 goals** focused on hitting revenue targets, board reporting, self-serve analytics, and building data culture
- **6 frustrations** covering analyst bottleneck, SQL-dependent tools, manual board prep, metric inconsistency, vendor trust issues, and consultant dependency
- **7 behaviors** detailing dashboard habits, purchasing patterns, evaluation methods, information sources, communication preferences, and social media influence
- **8-tool tech stack** with usage context for each tool
- **12-hour day-in-the-life narrative** from 6:30 AM to 10:00 PM with 12 time-stamped activities
- **5 direct quotes** capturing her voice and priorities
- **6 brand/influence references** showing products she admires, thought leaders she follows, and communities she belongs to
- **Full metadata** including market segment, revenue potential, buying triggers, and research sources

## Variations

1. **B2C Persona**: Replace company/role sections with lifestyle, hobbies, and media consumption. Add a "Typical Purchase Journey" section for consumer behavior.
2. **Negative Persona (Anti-Persona)**: Describe who your product is NOT for. Same structure but focused on why this person churns, complains, or never converts.
3. **Multi-Persona Comparison**: Create 3 persona boards side by side (Primary, Secondary, Tertiary) with a comparison matrix highlighting key differences.
4. **Lean Persona**: Reduce to Photo + 1 Goal + 1 Frustration + 1 Quote for teams that need a quick, lightweight reference.
5. **Data-Driven Persona**: Replace anecdotal content with quantitative segments from analytics data (cohort analysis, usage patterns, LTV segments).

## Tips & Best Practices

- Base every persona attribute on real research. If an attribute is an assumption, mark it with a question mark and validate it in the next research round.
- Give the persona a real name and photo (stock photo). Teams that use names ("What would Mia think?") build stronger empathy than those that reference segments ("What would the Series A CEO persona think?").
- The Day in the Life section is the most underrated part. It shows when and how your product fits into a real day, revealing timing, context, and competing priorities.
- Frustrations should be specific enough to inspire product features. "Analytics is hard" is not useful. "Board meeting prep takes 2 full days of manual data pulling" is actionable.
- Update the persona quarterly. As you learn more from customers, the persona should deepen and evolve.
- Share the persona board broadly — engineering, design, sales, support, and marketing should all know Mia by name.
- Use the persona in design reviews: "Would Mia find this onboarding confusing?" grounds abstract discussions in user reality.
- Do not create too many personas. 2-3 for a product is ideal. More than 5 means you have not prioritized your audience.
