# Examples & Reference — Team & HR Boards

## Overview

This document provides detailed breakdowns of exemplary Team and HR boards, common anti-patterns, before/after transformations, and design elements worth stealing for org charts, onboarding boards, team building workshops, and performance reviews.

---

## Example 1: NovaTech Org Chart (The Living Architecture)

### Board Summary

**Company**: NovaTech Inc. — B2B SaaS, Series C
**Headcount**: 187 employees across 6 departments
**Board Size**: 4800 x 3400px
**Palette**: Corporate Modern

### What Makes It Exceptional

**1. The Sidebar Is a Strategic Planning Tool, Not Just a Summary**

Most org chart sidebars show a simple headcount table. NovaTech's sidebar includes:

- Headcount by department (Current | Planned EOY | Open Roles)
- Key metrics: Span of control (6.2), Manager-to-IC ratio (1:5.4), Average tenure (2.8 years), Voluntary attrition (11.2%)
- Open positions prioritized by criticality (P0 Critical, P1 High, P2 Normal)
- Org health flags with specific recommendations

This transforms the sidebar from a reference panel into a strategic planning tool. A CHRO can look at the sidebar and immediately identify the top 3 structural issues to address.

**2. Dotted-Line Reports Show the Real Matrix**

Two dotted-line relationships are explicitly shown:

- Director of Customer Success → CRO (dotted-line for revenue alignment)
- Director of Eng Ops → CFO (dotted-line for infrastructure budget)

These dashed lines, in contrasting colors, reveal the matrix structure that solid-line charts hide. Without them, the chart implies Customer Success is purely a Product function, which is misleading.

**3. Org Health Flags Are Visible, Not Hidden**

Three pink sticky notes call out structural issues:

- AI/ML team: 12 ICs, 1 VP, no middle management layer → Hire an EM by Q2
- Customer Success: 10:1 span of control exceeds the 8:1 guideline → Promote a Team Lead
- Sales growing 15% but Sales Ops at 0% → Budget for 2 additional Sales Ops hires

These flags are the most valuable content on the entire board. They turn an org chart from a static snapshot into a diagnostic tool.

**4. Card Size and Color Hierarchy Reinforces Structure**

Without reading any text, the hierarchy is immediately visible:

- CEO card: 400x200px, dark navy (#0D47A1)
- C-Suite cards: 350x180px, full department color
- VP/Director cards: 300x160px, light department tint
- Team cards: 250x120px, lightest tint

The combination of decreasing size and decreasing color saturation creates a clear visual hierarchy that is readable at 30% zoom.

### Design Elements to Steal

- **Strategic sidebar**: Headcount delta, key metrics, prioritized open roles, and org health flags
- **Dotted-line visibility**: Matrix reporting shown with colored dashed lines
- **Org health flags**: Pink sticky notes calling out structural issues with recommendations
- **Size + saturation hierarchy**: Cards decrease in size AND color intensity by level

## Example 2: Maya's Onboarding Board (The Welcome Map)

### Board Summary

**New Hire**: Maya Johnson, Senior Product Designer
**Team**: Design (under Mia Chen, Head of Design)
**Duration**: 90 days (Day 1 through Month 3)
**Board Size**: 4600 x 3200px
**Palette**: Warm & Approachable

### What Makes It Exceptional

**1. Every Checklist Item Is Actionable Without Clarification**

Compare these two approaches:

- Generic: "Set up tools"
- NovaTech: "Set up design tools: Figma (team workspace invite), Miro (board access), Jira (design project board)"

Every item specifies what, where, and often who helps. Maya can complete her entire first week without asking "what does this mean?"

**2. Manager and Buddy Notes Add a Human Voice**

Throughout the board, personal sticky notes from Mia (manager) and Alex (buddy) appear:

- "Mia's note: Don't worry about being productive on Day 1. Focus on meeting people and absorbing context."
- "Alex's tip: The 1:1s with cross-functional partners are the MOST valuable thing this week. Ask each person: 'What's the one thing you wish design did better?'"

These notes transform a checklist into a conversation. They communicate that real humans prepared this board and care about Maya's experience.

**3. The 30-60-90 Goals Have "Success Signals"**

Each milestone period ends with a success signal — a concrete indicator that the milestone has been achieved:

- 30 days: "Mia trusts you to run a design critique and present to stakeholders independently"
- 60 days: "Cross-functional partners proactively seek your input on design decisions"
- 90 days: "You feel like a fully integrated member of the team, not 'the new person'"

These signals are both objective (running a critique) and emotional (feeling integrated). They give Maya a way to self-assess beyond checking boxes.

**4. The Color Progression Signals Maturation**

The four timeline lanes use a color progression that mirrors the new hire's journey:

- Pre-boarding/Day 1: Warm amber (#EF6C00) — excitement, orientation
- Week 1: Sage green (#388E3C) — learning, observation
- Week 2: Deep purple (#7B1FA2) — contribution, practice
- Weeks 3-4: Dark teal (#00695C) — ownership, delivery

This is not just aesthetic — the colors darken and deepen, subtly communicating that the work becomes more substantial over time.

### Design Elements to Steal

- **Actionable checklist items**: Every item includes what, where, and who
- **Human voice sticky notes**: Manager and buddy notes throughout
- **Success signals**: Concrete indicators at each milestone
- **Color maturation**: Phase colors deepen as the new hire's contribution deepens

## Example 3: Design Team Workshop (The Collaborative Canvas)

### Board Summary

**Team**: NovaTech Design Team (8 people)
**Facilitator**: Mia Chen, Head of Design
**Session**: Half-day team building workshop
**Board Size**: 4000 x 3000px
**Palette**: Warm & Approachable

### What Makes It Exceptional

**1. Every Team Member's Voice Is Present**

Across the six frames, every person's name appears at least 4 times:

- Values frame: 2 sticky notes per person (16 total contributions)
- Strengths frame: 1 card per person (8 cards)
- Fun facts frame: 1 note per person (8 notes)
- Preferences frame: 1 row per person in the matrix

If any person's name were missing, the board would feel exclusive. The design ensures comprehensive representation.

**2. The Strengths Map Identifies Team Gaps**

Beyond listing individual strengths, the board includes a "Strength Gaps Identified" section with two pink sticky notes:

- "No one listed 'service design' or 'journey mapping' — consider training or hiring for this skill"
- "Only 1 person strong in accessibility (Jordan) — build team-wide accessibility literacy"

Turning individual strengths into a team capability map, then identifying gaps, transforms a feel-good exercise into strategic team development.

**3. Working Agreements Are Specific and Enforceable**

Each agreement includes an enforcement mechanism or fallback:

- "If you can't attend, post your work in #design-critique by noon for async feedback" (alternative when the rule cannot be followed)
- "When you disagree with feedback, say why — not just 'I prefer my version.' Bring rationale" (defines what good disagreement looks like)
- "No Slack messages after 6 PM or before 8 AM unless it's truly urgent. Use scheduled send." (includes the specific tool to use)

### Design Elements to Steal

- **Universal participation**: Every team member's name appears in every activity
- **Gap analysis from strengths**: Individual strengths aggregated into team capabilities with gaps identified
- **Enforceable agreements**: Each rule includes what to do when the rule cannot be followed
- **Action items with owners**: Every action has a person and a date, not just a vague "we should"

## Example 4: Alex Kim's Performance Review (The Growth Map)

### Board Summary

**Employee**: Alex Kim, Senior Product Designer (IC4)
**Period**: H2 2025 (July-December)
**Rating**: 4.2 / 5.0 — Exceeds Expectations
**Board Size**: 5200 x 3600px
**Palette**: Corporate Modern

### What Makes It Exceptional

**1. The Development Plan Is the Largest Visual Element**

While the Overall Rating frame gets a large badge, the Development Plan frame is the most content-rich frame on the board. It contains three development areas, each with 3-5 specific actions, timelines, and manager support commitments. This visual emphasis communicates that the purpose of a review is growth, not grading.

**2. 360 Feedback Is Synthesized Into Themes, Not Dumped as Raw Quotes**

Instead of listing 15 individual comments, the feedback is organized into:

- 3 strength themes (with 1 representative quote each)
- 3 growth themes (with 1 representative quote each)
- 1 highlighted "verbatim of note" — the CPO's quote about Alex being a "quiet force"

This synthesis respects the reviewer's time while ensuring the key messages land clearly.

**3. The Career Path Includes a Fork Decision**

Rather than showing a single linear path, the career section shows a fork:

```
[Principal Designer IC6]    [Design Manager M1]
          \                    /
           [FORK — decide by mid-2027]
                  |
        [Staff Designer IC5]    ← TARGET: H2 2026
                  |
    [Senior Designer IC4]       ← CURRENT
```

This acknowledges that careers are not linear and that Alex has a meaningful choice to make. The board captures his current preference (IC track) while leaving the door open.

**4. Promotion Readiness Is Quantified**

A readiness table shows exactly where Alex stands against the 6 criteria for Staff Designer:

- 4 of 6: Ready (green)
- 2 of 6: Developing, 6-12 months (amber)
- Summary: "Expected readiness: Q3-Q4 2026"

This removes the mystery from promotion. Alex knows exactly what he needs to develop and approximately when he can expect to be ready.

### Design Elements to Steal

- **Development as the visual centerpiece**: More content and richer design in the forward-looking frames
- **Feedback synthesis**: Themes with representative quotes, not raw feedback dumps
- **Career fork visualization**: Showing IC vs management split as a visual fork
- **Quantified promotion readiness**: Criteria table with Ready / Developing status per criterion

## Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Title-Only Org Chart

**What it looks like**: Rectangles with "CTO," "VP Engineering," "Director," but no names, no headcounts, no context. Just boxes and lines.

**Why it fails**: It shows the architecture but not the people. It is useful for understanding the shape of the organization but useless for finding, contacting, or planning around actual humans.

**The fix**: Every card should include: name, title, department, headcount (for managers), and tenure. The sidebar should include aggregate metrics, open roles, and health flags.

### Anti-Pattern 2: The Copy-Paste Onboarding

**What it looks like**: An onboarding board that says "[New Hire Name]" and "[Manager Name]" in placeholder brackets. Tasks are generic: "Complete training," "Meet team."

**Why it fails**: The new hire immediately sees that nobody took the time to customize their experience. It signals that the company treats onboarding as an afterthought.

**The fix**: Personalize every board. Fill in the real name, real manager, real buddy, real tools, real tasks, and real 30-60-90 goals specific to the role.

### Anti-Pattern 3: The Annual Surprise

**What it looks like**: A performance review where the employee learns about a "Below Expectations" rating for the first time during the review meeting. No prior feedback, no warning, no documentation.

**Why it fails**: If the first time someone hears about a performance issue is during the formal review, the manager has failed at ongoing feedback. Reviews should summarize what has already been discussed, not reveal new information.

**The fix**: The review board should reference previous 1:1 discussions, documented feedback, and intermediate check-ins. The goals frame should show check-in dates. Nothing on the board should be a surprise.

### Anti-Pattern 4: The Feedback Firehose

**What it looks like**: A performance review that dumps 20+ raw 360 feedback quotes onto the board with no organization, no themes, no synthesis.

**Why it fails**: The employee cannot extract meaning from raw data. They may fixate on one negative comment and miss the overall positive pattern. The lack of synthesis makes the feedback feel like surveillance rather than development.

**The fix**: Synthesize feedback into 3-4 strength themes and 2-3 growth themes. Select 1-2 representative quotes per theme. Attribute manager feedback; anonymize peer and direct report feedback.

### Anti-Pattern 5: The Dead Workshop Board

**What it looks like**: A beautifully designed team building board with sticky notes from a workshop 6 months ago. No action items were completed. The working agreements were never referenced again.

**Why it fails**: A workshop without follow-through is worse than no workshop. Team members become cynical about future team building efforts.

**The fix**: Schedule a follow-up within 2 weeks to review action items. Reference the board in team standups. Set a quarterly review date to revisit and update working agreements.

### Anti-Pattern 6: The Flattering Review

**What it looks like**: Every goal is rated "Exceeded," every competency is "Above Level," and the narrative is all praise. No development areas, no growth feedback.

**Why it fails**: Even the highest performers have development areas. A review with no constructive feedback provides no direction for growth and feels dishonest.

**The fix**: Every review must include at least 2 growth areas with specific development actions, even for top performers. Frame these as "the gap between where you are and where you could be."

## Before / After Transformations

### Before: Generic Org Chart

```
[CEO]
  |
[CTO]  [CRO]  [CPO]  [CFO]
  |       |      |      |
[Teams] [Teams] [Teams] [Teams]
```

### After: Living Org Chart

```
[Linda Park, CEO — Reports to Board]
  |
[Marcus Wong, CTO — Eng, 72 ppl]  [James Mitchell, CRO — Sales, 48 ppl]  ...
  |                                  |
[VP Backend: Raj, 28]              [VP Sales: Jim, 28]
[VP Frontend: Yuki, 22]           [VP Marketing: Priya, 14]
[VP AI/ML: Fatima, 12 (NEW)]     [Dir Sales Ops: Tomoko, 6]
  |
[Platform: Ana, 8] [API: Omar, 7] [Data: Sofia, 7] [Security: Kwame, 6]

SIDEBAR: Headcount 187/214 planned | 27 open roles | 3 org health flags
```

### Before: Generic Review Rating

```
Overall Rating: 4.2 / 5.0
Comment: Good performance this half.
```

### After: Evidence-Based Review Rating

```
OVERALL RATING: 4.2 / 5.0 — EXCEEDS EXPECTATIONS

Goals: 2 Exceeded, 4 Met, 2 Partially Met (partial due to external blocker)
Competencies: 4/8 above level, 3/8 at level, 1/8 below level (strategic thinking)
360 Feedback: 4.8 cross-functional rating (highest on design team)
Highlight: Checkout redesign delivered 22% conversion improvement

MANAGER NARRATIVE: Alex had an outstanding H2 2025. The checkout redesign was
the single most impactful design project of the half. His mentorship of Sam was
exemplary. The primary development area is strategic thinking — Alex needs to
transition from 'best executor' to 'strategic design leader.'

COMPENSATION: 6% merit (vs 4% standard) + 500 RSUs + $3K spot bonus
```

### Before: Flat Working Agreement

```
- Be respectful
- Communicate openly
- Do good work
- Attend meetings
```

### After: Specific Working Agreement

```
1. Design critiques: Wed 2 PM, non-negotiable. If absent, post work in
   #design-critique by noon for async feedback.
2. Figma comments: respond within 24 hours. If you need more time, reply
   "acknowledged, will address by [date]."
3. No pixel-perfect feedback on exploration-phase work. Explorations are
   for direction, not polish.
4. Disagree with data: when you push back on feedback, bring user data,
   design principles, or technical constraints — not just preference.
5. Pair-design once per sprint. Rotate partners quarterly.
6. No Slack after 6 PM / before 8 AM. Use scheduled send.
7. Blocked for 2+ days? Escalate to Mia. Don't suffer in silence.
8. Celebrate launches: every ship gets a #design-wins post with credit.
```
