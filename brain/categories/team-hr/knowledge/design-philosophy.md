# Design Philosophy — Team & HR Boards

## Overview

Team and HR boards visualize the invisible infrastructure of organizations: reporting relationships, cultural norms, growth trajectories, and the rituals that turn a group of individuals into a functioning team. Unlike boards that visualize data or processes, these boards visualize _people_ — and people require a design philosophy that balances structure with humanity, professionalism with warmth, and clarity with nuance.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. People Are Not Boxes

The fundamental tension in organizational design is that we represent humans as rectangles. A role card, a team node, a performance rating — these are abstractions of complex, multidimensional people. The design philosophy must counteract the dehumanizing tendency of organizational charts by:

- **Including names, not just titles**: "Marcus Wong, CTO" is a person. "CTO" is a function. The board should always feel like it is about people, not positions.
- **Using warm color palettes**: Corporate blue is professional, but used alone it feels cold. Every Team/HR board should include warm accent colors (amber, coral, green) to communicate that this is about humans, not machinery.
- **Allowing imperfection**: Team building boards, in particular, should feel collaborative and slightly informal — like a whiteboard after a good workshop, not a corporate slide deck.
- **Including voice**: Quotes, personal notes, fun facts, and individual perspectives make boards feel human. A sticky note that says "Mia's note: Don't worry about being productive on Day 1" communicates more care than a hundred checkboxes.

### 2. Structure Reveals Power

Every organizational visualization is inherently political. Who is at the top? Who reports to whom? Who is grouped together? These are not neutral design choices — they communicate authority, hierarchy, and influence. The design philosophy must:

- **Make hierarchy explicit but not oppressive**: Top-down org charts clearly show reporting lines, but the visual weight should not make the CEO card 10x larger than an IC card. Use subtle size differences (350px vs 250px), not dramatic ones.
- **Show dotted-line relationships**: Matrix reporting is the reality of modern organizations. Hiding it gives a false picture. Dashed lines for dotted-line reports and cross-functional connections show the real structure.
- **Acknowledge structural issues**: Org health flags, span-of-control warnings, and gap annotations are not problems to hide — they are insights to surface. A good org chart should make structural weaknesses visible so they can be addressed.
- **Use department colors consistently**: When Engineering is green and Sales is orange across every board, people build instant recognition. Consistency across boards is more important than any single board's aesthetic preference.

### 3. Time Is the Hidden Dimension

Team/HR boards operate across multiple time horizons simultaneously:

- **The org chart is a snapshot**: It shows structure _right now_. But organizations change constantly — hires, departures, reorgs. The design must communicate currency ("Effective: March 1, 2026") and acknowledge planned changes ("Open Roles: 27").
- **Onboarding unfolds over weeks**: A Day 1 checklist, Week 1 tasks, and 30-60-90 day goals create a temporal arc. The design must make this progression visible through spatial arrangement (left-to-right) and color progression.
- **Performance reviews bridge past and future**: The left side of a review board looks backward (goals, achievements), the center evaluates the present (ratings, feedback), and the right side looks forward (development plan, career path). This temporal flow should be visually obvious.
- **Team dynamics evolve**: A team building board captures a moment in time, but working agreements and values should feel durable. Use permanent-feeling design elements (solid borders, clean type) for agreements, and ephemeral-feeling elements (sticky notes, handwritten style) for brainstorming contributions.

### 4. Confidentiality Shapes Design

HR boards frequently contain sensitive information: performance ratings, compensation data, feedback attribution, and career discussions. The design philosophy must:

- **Signal confidentiality visually**: A red italic notice ("Confidential — Do not share outside of the review participants") is not just legal protection — it is a visual cue that this board matters and should be treated with care.
- **Support anonymization**: 360-degree feedback should attribute quotes only where appropriate (manager feedback can be attributed; peer feedback should be anonymized by default). The design should make it easy to include feedback without revealing sources.
- **Separate shareable from private**: Some sections of a board (goals, development plan) may be shared with the employee, while others (calibration notes, compensation recommendations) are manager-only. Use visual boundaries (distinct frames, different backgrounds) to separate these zones.
- **Avoid comparative layouts by default**: Displaying multiple employees' performance reviews side-by-side invites comparison and can feel dehumanizing. Each review should be its own board. Comparison is for calibration sessions, which have their own design requirements.

### 5. Boards Should Be Working Documents, Not Artifacts

A common mistake is designing HR boards as finished presentations. In practice:

- **Onboarding boards should be interactive**: The new hire checks off items, adds notes, and makes the board their own. Design for interaction, not observation.
- **Team building boards should be collaborative**: Participants add sticky notes during workshops. Leave generous empty space for contributions.
- **Org charts should be updatable**: The board should be easy to modify when someone is hired, promoted, or departs. Avoid designs that require rebuilding the entire board to add one person.
- **Performance reviews should prompt conversation**: The board is a framework for a 1:1 discussion, not a report to be filed. Include prompts, questions, and open-ended sections alongside structured evaluations.

## The Four Board Types — Design Intent

### Org Chart: The Architecture of Authority

**Design intent**: Clarity and navigation. Someone should be able to find any person or team within 10 seconds.

**Key design decisions**:

- Top-down hierarchy (most common and most intuitive for Western audiences)
- Department color coding creates visual clusters
- Decreasing card size by level (CEO largest, team cards smallest) communicates hierarchy without text
- Sidebar contains aggregate data (headcount, metrics, flags) that does not fit in the tree
- Connector line thickness decreases by level (3px, 2px, 1px) — another hierarchy signal

**Non-negotiables**:

- Every person has a name, not just a title
- Headcount numbers must sum correctly (team counts must equal department totals)
- Open roles are visible (the chart shows where the organization is going, not just where it is)
- Structural health flags are surfaced, not hidden

### Onboarding Board: The Welcome Map

**Design intent**: Guidance and psychological safety. A new hire should feel supported, informed, and excited — not overwhelmed.

**Key design decisions**:

- Left-to-right temporal flow (pre-boarding → Day 1 → Week 1 → Week 2 → Weeks 3-4)
- Color progression signals maturation (warm amber for early days → sage green for learning → deep purple for contributing → teal for ownership)
- Checkboxes create a sense of progress (the new hire sees items move from unchecked to checked)
- Manager and buddy notes add personal warmth to an otherwise structured checklist
- 30-60-90 day goals create a bridge from tactical onboarding to long-term performance

**Non-negotiables**:

- The board is personalized (name, role, team, manager, buddy — not a generic template)
- Cultural context is included (values, norms, communication expectations)
- The first day is not dominated by paperwork — it prioritizes human connection
- There is a clear escalation path ("If you are stuck, tell your buddy. If it is bigger, tell your manager.")

### Team Building Board: The Workshop Canvas

**Design intent**: Collaboration and energy. The board should feel like a room full of sticky notes after a great workshop — organized enough to be useful, informal enough to be inviting.

**Key design decisions**:

- Six-frame grid with distinct activity zones
- Each frame has its own background color tint — this creates visual variety and signals that each activity is different
- Sticky notes dominate (not shapes or text blocks) — they feel participatory and temporary
- The board starts with values and strengths (generative, positive) and ends with action items (concrete, committed)
- A "fun facts" section exists purely for bonding — not everything needs to be productive

**Non-negotiables**:

- Every team member's voice appears on the board (if one person's name is missing, the board fails)
- Working agreements are commitments, not suggestions — they use definitive language
- Action items have owners and due dates (not "we should..." but "Jordan will... by March 21")
- There is a follow-up mechanism (when will the team revisit this board?)

### Performance Review Board: The Growth Map

**Design intent**: Fairness, clarity, and forward momentum. The employee should leave the review conversation knowing exactly where they stand, what they need to develop, and what the path forward looks like.

**Key design decisions**:

- Six-frame grid flowing from past (left) through present (center) to future (right)
- Goals and competencies provide the factual foundation (left column)
- Feedback and overall rating provide the evaluation (center column)
- Development plan and career path provide the motivation (right column)
- The largest visual element is the development plan, not the rating — this communicates that growth matters more than grades

**Non-negotiables**:

- Every rating has evidence (examples, data, quotes — never a number without context)
- The employee's self-assessment is included (this is a dialogue, not a verdict)
- Development areas connect to career goals (if the employee cannot see why they should develop strategic thinking, they will not)
- Confidentiality boundaries are explicit (who can see this board, what can be shared)

## Design Tensions and How to Resolve Them

### Warmth vs. Professionalism

**Tension**: HR boards need to feel human and warm, but they also need to be taken seriously as business documents.

**Resolution**: Use warm colors as accents, not as primaries. The primary color is always corporate blue (#1565C0). Warm colors (amber, green, coral) appear in section highlights, sticky notes, and badges. Text is always in charcoal (#212121) — never in a warm color for body text. This creates a board that reads as professional at 30% zoom but feels warm when you read the content at 100%.

### Structure vs. Flexibility

**Tension**: Org charts need rigid structure (hierarchy demands it), but team building boards need open space for participation.

**Resolution**: Use frames to create structure, but vary the density. Org charts fill 80-90% of their frame space with elements. Team building boards fill 40-50%, leaving room for participant contributions. Onboarding boards start dense (pre-populated checklists) and become sparser as the timeline extends (30-60-90 goals are broader, less prescriptive).

### Individual vs. Aggregate

**Tension**: Org charts show aggregate structure (departments, teams, headcounts), but HR boards serve individuals (one person's review, one person's onboarding).

**Resolution**: Use sidebars and summary panels for aggregate data, and main body content for individual detail. The org chart sidebar shows headcount totals and org health metrics. The performance review board's main frames focus on one person's experience. Never mix aggregate and individual data in the same visual zone.

### Current State vs. Planned State

**Tension**: An org chart shows who is here now, but hiring managers need to see where the organization is going.

**Resolution**: Use the sidebar for planned headcount and open roles. In the main tree, mark new/planned positions with a distinct visual treatment (dashed borders, "PLANNED" badges, or a different shade of the department color). The current state dominates the visual hierarchy; the planned state is visible but secondary.
