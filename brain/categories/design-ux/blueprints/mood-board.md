# Mood Board

## Overview

- **Purpose**: Gather and organize visual inspiration to establish the aesthetic direction for a product, feature, or campaign before any design work begins. Includes color swatches, reference images, texture samples, font pairings, mood descriptors, and competitive visual references. Aligns the team on "how it should feel" before "how it should look."
- **Best For**: Design teams kicking off a new project, brand managers exploring a visual refresh, marketing teams defining campaign aesthetics, and stakeholders providing visual direction feedback.
- **Complexity**: Simple
- **Board Size**: 4000x3000px

## Connectors & Flow

- No flow connectors — this is an inspiration board, not a process diagram.
- Frames are positioned with slight overlaps and varied sizes to create a collage aesthetic rather than a rigid grid.
- Sticky note annotations add conversational context to each section, mimicking real team feedback.

## Example Content

The mood board explores the visual direction for AutoPilot 3.0 with a curated color palette (3 primary + 4 accent options with team feedback), 6 image reference placeholders with detailed descriptions, 4 texture/pattern samples, 3 font pairing options (with decisions marked), a competitive visual positioning map with 7 products plotted, and 5 positive mood descriptors + 3 "not this" anti-patterns.

## Variations

1. **Campaign Mood Board**: Replace product UI references with campaign-specific imagery (photography style, ad layouts, video stills). Add a "Key Visual Concept" frame with hero image direction.
2. **Brand Refresh Mood Board**: Add frames for "Current Brand" (before) and "Target Direction" (after) to show the evolution. Include competitor brand comparisons.
3. **Physical Product Mood Board**: Add material samples (wood, metal, fabric textures), packaging references, and unboxing experience imagery.

## Build Instructions

1. Create board at 4000x3000px with background #FFF8F0 (warm white).
2. Place Frame 1 (Title) at (50, 50), size 3900x200, background #263238.
3. Place Frame 2 (Colors) at (50, 300), size 1200x1000.
4. Place Frame 3 (Images) at (1200, 280), size 1600x1400 — intentionally overlapping Frame 2 slightly.
5. Place Frame 4 (Textures) at (2850, 300), size 1000x1000.
6. Place Frame 5 (Fonts) at (50, 1350), size 1200x800.
7. Place Frame 6 (Competitors) at (1300, 1700), size 1200x800.
8. Place Frame 7 (Descriptors) at (2600, 1400), size 1000x800.
9. Apply subtle drop shadows to all frames to create depth.
10. Position image placeholders at varied sizes within Frame 3 for a collage feel.
11. Add sticky note annotations with realistic team feedback.

## Tips & Best Practices

- This board is for exploration, not decision-making. Let it be messy and abundant — you can always refine later.
- Include team member annotations (sticky notes with names and opinions) to capture the discussion that happens around visual direction.
- Show rejected options (with strikethrough or "rejected" labels) — they document why you chose the direction you chose.
- Use this board at the kickoff of any new design project. 30 minutes of mood boarding saves days of going in the wrong visual direction.
- After the mood board is finalized, it feeds directly into the brand-guidelines.md and design-system.md boards for formal documentation.
