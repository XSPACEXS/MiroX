# Workshop Boards — Content Guide

## Overview

Workshop board content exists in two layers: the facilitation layer (instructions, prompts,
rules, timing) and the participant layer (ideas, votes, decisions, actions). The facilitator
layer is written before the workshop and locked. The participant layer is generated during
the workshop and represents the actual output. This guide covers how to write excellent
facilitation content — the text that guides participants through each activity and shapes
the quality of their contributions.

| Section           | Required?   | Key Questions Answered                             |
| ----------------- | ----------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Problem Statement | Required    | What are we brainstorming about? What constraints? |
| Context/Data      | Required    | What does the team need to know before generating? |
| Silent Ideation   | Required    | Where do participants write ideas? How many?       |
| Clustering/Themes | Required    | How are ideas grouped? Who groups them?            |
| Voting            | Required    | How many votes? What criteria?                     |
| Action Plan       | Required    | What happens next? Who owns what?                  |
| Rules             | Required    | What are the brainstorm ground rules?              |
| Parking Lot       | Recommended | Where do off-topic but valuable ideas go?          |
| Timeline          | Required    | How long is each phase?                            |

### Retrospective — Essential Content

| Section                | Required?   | Key Questions Answered                                      |
| ---------------------- | ----------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Sprint/Period Info     | Required    | What period are we reflecting on? What was the goal?        |
| Previous Retro Actions | Required    | What did we commit to last time? Did we do it?              |

| Facilitator Guide      | Required    | Full agenda with timing                                     |
| Parking Lot            | Recommended | Issues to escalate or discuss elsewhere                     |

## Writing Guidelines

### Workshop Instruction Voice

Workshop instructions should be:

- **Direct and action-oriented**: "Write one idea per sticky note" not "Participants
  should consider writing their ideas on individual sticky notes."
- **Specific about quantity**: "Generate at least 5 ideas" not "Generate ideas."
- **Clear about timing**: "5 minutes — silent writing" not "Take a few minutes to
  think about this."
- **Encouraging without being patronizing**: "All ideas are welcome during this phase"
  not "Remember, there are no bad ideas! Every idea is special!"
- **Inclusive in language**: "Each person gets 3 votes" not "Each guy gets 3 votes."
  "Write your response" not "Raise your hand and share."

### The Instruction Formula

Every activity instruction on a workshop board should follow this formula:

```
[Activity Name] — (Duration: X min)
[One sentence explaining what to do]
[One sentence explaining the rules or constraints]
[One sentence explaining why this activity matters — optional but powerful]
```

**Example — Good**:

```
Silent Ideation — (10 min)
Write one idea per sticky note. Aim for at least 5 ideas each.
Rules: No talking, no judging, quantity over quality.
Why: Silent writing ensures all voices contribute, not just the loudest.
```

**Example — Bad**:

```
Ideation
Please begin brainstorming. Write down any ideas you might have about the topic
we discussed. Feel free to use sticky notes. Take your time.
```

The good example is actionable, bounded, and transparent. The bad example is vague,
open-ended, and provides no structure.

### The Problem Statement Formula

The quality of a brainstorm depends entirely on the quality of the problem statement.
Use this formula:

```
How might we [desired change] for [target user/audience]
while [honoring constraint 1] and [honoring constraint 2]?

Context:
- [Key data point 1 — quantitative if possible]
- [Key data point 2]
- [Key data point 3]

Success metric: [Specific, measurable outcome]
Constraints: [Non-negotiable boundaries]
```

**Example**:

```
How might we reduce the 62% drop-off rate during user onboarding (steps 3-5)
while maintaining data quality for our enterprise clients?

Context:
- Current onboarding has 7 steps. Drop-off spikes at Step 3 and Step 5.
- Enterprise clients need detailed profiles for compliance.
- Mobile is 40% of traffic but the flow is not optimized for small screens.

Success metric: Reduce drop-off from 62% to under 40% within 90 days.
Constraints: Cannot remove required fields for enterprise tier.
                Must maintain SOC2 compliance. 2 sprints max capacity.
```

### The Retrospective Question Formula

Retrospective questions should be:

- Open-ended enough to invite diverse responses
- Specific enough to focus reflection on actionable topics
- Emotionally safe to answer honestly

**Metaphor-based questions (Sailboat)**:

```
Wind: "What pushed us forward this sprint? What felt good?"
Anchor: "What dragged us down? What frustrated the team?"
Rocks: "What risks or dangers do we see ahead?"
Island: "What does our ideal state look like? What are we sailing toward?"
```

**Direct questions (Start-Stop-Continue)**:

```
Start: "What should we START doing that we are not doing now?"
Stop: "What should we STOP doing because it is not working?"
Continue: "What should we CONTINUE because it is working well?"
```

**Emotional questions (Mad-Sad-Glad)**:

```
Mad: "What made you angry or frustrated this sprint?"
Sad: "What disappointed you or made you feel down?"
Glad: "What made you happy, proud, or grateful?"
```

### The Action Item Formula (SMART)

Every workshop action item must follow the SMART format:

```
Owner: [Name]
Action: [Specific, concrete thing to do — verb first]
Category: [Which theme/insight this addresses]
Due Date: [Specific date]
Status: [To Do / In Progress / Done]
```

**Example — Good**:

```
Owner: Lin
Action: Fix the top 5 flaky integration tests in the staging environment
Category: Anchor (flaky tests were top-voted blocker)
Due Date: March 13, 2026
Status: To Do
```

**Example — Bad**:

```
Owner: Team
Action: Improve test reliability
Due Date: Soon
```

The good example is assignable, specific, dated, and traceable to the workshop
insight. The bad example is vague, unassignable, and undated — it will never happen.

### The World Cafe Table Question Formula

Table questions for World Cafe discussions should be:

- **Open enough for 15 minutes of diverse discussion**: Not answerable in one sentence
- **Provocative enough to generate disagreement**: Polite consensus produces shallow insights
- **Connected to the Big Question**: Each table explores a facet of the overarching theme
- **Actionable**: The discussion should naturally produce ideas for what to DO, not just think

```
[Topic Domain]: "[Provocative question that creates productive tension
and connects back to the Big Question]"
```

**Example**:

```
Culture & Values: "What cultural values have made us successful —
and which ones are becoming obstacles as we scale?"
```

This question is powerful because it acknowledges success (not defensive) while
demanding honest assessment of what must change. It cannot be answered in one sentence.
It will generate disagreement (people will disagree about which values are obstacles).
And it naturally leads to action ("If X value is becoming an obstacle, what do we do?").

## Real Content Examples

### Example: Brainstorm Ground Rules

```
Brainstorm Rules:
1. Defer judgment — all ideas are welcome during ideation
2. Go for quantity — aim for volume, not perfection
3. Build on others' ideas — "Yes, and..." not "No, but..."
4. One conversation at a time during discussion rounds
5. Stay visual — draw, sketch, use images when possible
6. Be bold — wild ideas are encouraged, they spark practical ones
```

### Example: Retrospective Sprint Information

```
Sprint 14 Retrospective
Team: Platform Engineering | Sprint: Feb 17 - Mar 6, 2026
Facilitator: Sarah Chen | Participants: 8 | Duration: 45 min
Sprint Goal: "Ship the new billing API and migrate 3 enterprise clients"
Ground rules: One voice at a time | No blame | Focus on systems, not people | Vegas rule
```

### Example: Design Thinking Empathy Interview Questions

```
Empathy Interview Questions:
1. Tell me about the last time you visited a doctor's office. Walk me through
   the check-in from the parking lot to the waiting room.
2. What was the most frustrating moment? What were you feeling?
3. If you could wave a magic wand and change one thing about check-in,
   what would it be?
4. How do you currently prepare for a doctor visit? What do you bring?
5. Have you ever had a great check-in experience anywhere? What made it great?
```

### Example: World Cafe Harvest Wall Categories

```
HARVEST WALL — Collective Insights

Key Insights:
- Our founding values are right but the practices must evolve for scale
- The next growth wave is platform + self-serve + AI-native
- People strategy IS business strategy

Cross-Table Patterns:
- PATTERN: "Sustainable pace" appeared in Culture, People, AND Product tables
- PATTERN: "Focus and let go" — every table identified things to stop doing

Biggest Surprises:
- Leadership wants MORE structure, not less
- Every table mentioned AI — it is a culture, hiring, AND strategy question

Action Seeds:
- Values refresh workshop — Q2, led by Elena
- AI-native squad — 6 engineers, 2 designers, 90-day mission
- Self-serve tier pilot in healthcare — Q3
```

### Example: Icebreaker Activity Instructions

```
Two Truths and a Lie
Write 2 true facts and 1 lie about yourself. Others guess the lie! (10-15 min)

How to play:
1. Write your 3 statements on sticky notes (no telling which is the lie!)
2. Read them aloud to the group
3. Group votes on which statement is the lie
4. Reveal the answer!

Scoring (optional): 1 point for each correct guess. 1 bonus point if nobody
guesses your lie.
```

## Questions Every Workshop Board Should Answer

### For Any Viewer (3-second scan)

1. What is this workshop about?
2. How long is it?
3. What are the major phases?

### For a Participant (30-second orientation)

4. What am I supposed to do right now?
5. Where do I put my contributions?
6. How much time do I have?

### For the Facilitator (full reference)

7. What is the exact agenda with timing for each phase?
8. What are the probing questions for each discussion?
9. What are the ground rules?
10. What should I say to transition between phases?
11. What do I do if we run over time?
12. Where is the parking lot for off-topic items?

### For Someone Who Missed the Workshop (post-session review)

13. What problem was being addressed?
14. What ideas were generated?
15. How were those ideas evaluated?
16. What decisions were made?
17. What actions were committed to, with owners and deadlines?

## Data and Metrics on Workshop Boards

### During the Workshop

Workshop boards should capture quantitative data as the session progresses:

- **Idea count**: "15 ideas generated by 12 participants"
- **Vote totals**: "36 total votes from 12 participants (3 each)"
- **Theme count**: "4 clusters identified"
- **Action count**: "5 actions committed, all assigned, all time-bound"
- **Previous action status**: "2 of 4 previous actions completed, 1 in progress, 1 deferred"

These numbers serve multiple purposes:

1. They validate that the workshop produced tangible output
2. They create a sense of accomplishment during the session
3. They provide data for comparing workshops over time

### Post-Workshop Metrics

For recurring workshops (retrospectives, brainstorms), track these metrics across sessions:

| Metric                     | What It Measures                                |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Ideas per participant      | Engagement level of the group                   |
| Actions completed rate     | Follow-through from previous sessions           |
| Repeat issues count        | Whether the team is actually solving problems   |
| Participation equity       | Whether all members contribute roughly equally  |
| Session satisfaction (1-5) | Whether participants find the workshop valuable |
| Time-to-action             | How quickly committed actions are started       |

### The "3-Retro Rule"

If the same issue appears in 3 consecutive retrospectives, it is a systemic problem
that the team cannot solve alone. The facilitator should escalate it:

```
ESCALATION TRIGGER:
"Flaky integration tests" appeared in Sprint 12, 13, AND 14 retros.
This is a systemic issue requiring engineering leadership attention.
Recommended: Schedule a dedicated engineering review with the infra team.
```

## Content Density by Board Type

Different workshop types have different content density needs:

| Board Type      | Pre-filled Content  | Participant Content           | Ratio |
| --------------- | ------------------- | ----------------------------- | ----- |
| Brainstorm      | Problem + rules     | Ideas + votes + actions       | 30/70 |
| Retrospective   | Categories + prev   | Reflections + votes + actions | 25/75 |
| Design Thinking | Prompts + templates | Research + ideas + prototypes | 35/65 |
| World Cafe      | Questions + guides  | Discussion + harvest          | 30/70 |
| Icebreaker      | Activities + rules  | Responses + photos            | 40/60 |

The general rule: pre-filled content should never exceed 40% of the board. The
majority of space is reserved for what participants create during the session.

## Facilitator Guide Content

Every workshop board should include a locked facilitator guide panel with:

1. **Full agenda with timing**: Every phase, every activity, every minute accounted for
2. **Opening script**: What to say to set the context and energy
3. **Transition phrases**: What to say when moving between phases
4. **Probing questions**: Follow-up questions for when discussion stalls
5. **Time management**: What to cut if running over, what to extend if ahead
6. **Closing script**: How to end the session and commit to follow-up
7. **Post-session checklist**: Screenshot the board, send summary, schedule follow-up

### Example Facilitator Guide

```
Facilitator Guide — Brainstorm Session (60 min)

Agenda:
1. Set the stage — present problem statement (5 min)
2. Silent ideation — everyone writes (10 min)
3. Share round — brief read-aloud of ideas (5 min)
4. Cluster — group similar ideas into themes (10 min)
5. Dot vote — 3 votes per person on clusters (5 min)
6. Rank and discuss top clusters (10 min)
7. Define action items for top-voted themes (10 min)
8. Close and next steps (5 min)

If running over:
- Cut share round (step 3) — just move to clustering
- Reduce discussion (step 6) from 10 min to 5 min
- Do NOT cut action items — that is the whole point

If running ahead:
- Extend ideation to 15 min (more ideas = better output)
- Add a "build on others' ideas" round after sharing

Post-session:
- Screenshot the final board state
- Send summary to team within 48 hours
- Add committed actions to the team backlog/Jira
- Review actions at next retro
```
