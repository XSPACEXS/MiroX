# Examples & Reference — Education Boards

## Overview

This document provides detailed breakdowns of exemplary education boards, common anti-patterns, before/after transformations, and design elements worth stealing for lesson plans, research boards, study maps, and course curricula.

---

## Example 1: Grade 9 Biology Lesson Plan (The Gold Standard)

### Board Summary

**Subject**: Introduction to Photosynthesis
**Grade/Level**: Grade 9 Biology
**Duration**: 50 minutes
**Board Size**: 4000 x 2800px
**Palette**: Academic Classic

### What Makes It Exceptional

**1. The Activity Timeline Is Proportionally Accurate**

Each activity column is exactly proportional to its duration. The 15-minute Direct Instruction block is 3x wider than the 5-minute Hook. This means the teacher can glance at the board and immediately see that the middle of the lesson is instruction-heavy while the beginning and end are shorter, engagement-focused moments.

**2. Every Activity Has a Bloom's Badge**

Small colored badges in the top-right of each activity card show the cognitive level:

```
[HOOK: REMEMBER]  →  [DI: UNDERSTAND]  →  [PAIR: APPLY]  →  [LAB: APPLY]  →  [DISCUSS: ANALYZE]  →  [PREDICT: ANALYZE]
```

Scanning the badges left to right reveals a clear progression from Remember to Analyze. This is pedagogically rigorous and visually immediate.

**3. The Differentiation Row Shows Three Paths**

Below each major activity, a small visual fork shows three options:

- Blue path (scaffold): "Word bank provided for labeling activity"
- Green path (core): "Label diagram using textbook reference"
- Orange path (extension): "Label without references + draw energy flow arrows"

These are concise, actionable, and specific to each activity.

**4. Teacher Notes Include Timing Contingencies**

The yellow teacher note sticky notes do not just say what to do — they say what to do when things go wrong:

- "If Elodea demo takes >5 min for visible results, show backup time-lapse video (2 min)"
- "If more than 5 students give 'thumb down' after DI, re-explain using the simple word equation before moving to pairs"
- "If lab cleanup runs over, cut closing reflection to 2 minutes and assign the reflection as homework"

### Design Elements to Steal

- **Proportional activity widths**: Width = duration ratio, making pacing intuitive
- **Bloom's badge progression**: Small colored badges showing cognitive level progression left to right
- **Three-path differentiation forks**: Scaffold / Core / Extension below each major activity
- **Contingency teacher notes**: "If X happens, do Y" timing guidance

## Example 2: University Course Curriculum Board (The Semester Map)

### Board Summary

**Course**: Digital Marketing Strategy (Marketing 301)
**Level**: Undergraduate, 3rd year
**Duration**: 15-week semester
**Board Size**: 5500 x 3000px
**Palette**: EdTech Modern

### What Makes It Exceptional

**1. The Bloom's Row Reveals the Course Arc**

The bottom row of the grid shows the cognitive progression across the semester:

```
Wk1: REMEMBER → Wk3: UNDERSTAND → Wk5: APPLY → Wk8: ANALYZE → Wk11: EVALUATE → Wk14: CREATE
```

The colors darken from light blue (Remember) to deep red (Create). At a glance, you can see that the course starts with foundational knowledge and builds to creative application. This arc is the signature of a well-designed course.

**2. Assignment Milestones Are Visually Prominent**

Major assignments (Quiz 1, Midterm, Project Draft, Final) are displayed as larger, colored cards that break the grid rhythm. They use a gold background and are 50% taller than regular cells. This makes the assessment cadence visible at 30% zoom — you can see four "mountains" rising from the otherwise flat grid.

**3. The Bottom Panel Shows Cross-Week Dependencies**

Below the grid, a small flow diagram shows how assignments build on each other:

```
HW1 (Channel Audit) → HW3 (Email Sequence) → Project Draft → Final Project
```

This reveals that the course is not a series of isolated assignments but a progressive project where each homework contributes to the final deliverable.

### Design Elements to Steal

- **Bloom's row as color gradient**: Course cognitive arc visible as a color progression
- **Elevated assignment markers**: Major assessments taller and gold-highlighted, visible at low zoom
- **Cross-week dependency flow**: Shows how assignments build on each other

## Example 3: Doctoral Research Board (The Scholar's Canvas)

### Board Summary

**Study**: AI-adaptive learning platforms in Title I high schools
**Level**: Doctoral dissertation research
**Board Size**: 4500 x 3500px
**Palette**: Research Neutral

### What Makes It Exceptional

**1. The Literature Review Is a Visual Map, Not a List**

Instead of listing sources chronologically, the literature review arranges sources spatially:

- Central cluster: Sources directly studying AI in education
- Left cluster: Sources on adaptive learning theory
- Right cluster: Sources on Title I school contexts
- The GAP sits at the intersection of all three clusters, highlighted in amber

This spatial arrangement reveals the gap visually — it is literally the empty space where the three areas overlap.

**2. The Methodology Section Shows Validity Threats**

Rather than just listing the methodology, each design choice is annotated with its validity rationale:

- "Quasi-experimental (not randomized) — addresses ethical concerns of withholding treatment. THREAT: Selection bias. MITIGATION: Propensity score matching on 8 variables."
- "16-week duration — long enough for curriculum integration. THREAT: Maturation and history. MITIGATION: Pre-post control group design."

These annotations demonstrate methodological rigor without requiring a separate methods chapter.

**3. The Timeline Is Gated by Milestones**

The research timeline at the bottom uses a milestone-gated format:

```
[IRB Approval] ──→ [Pilot Study] ──→ [Data Collection] ──→ [Analysis] ──→ [Defense]
   Feb 2026          Apr 2026          Sep-Dec 2026        Jan-Mar 2027    May 2027
```

Each gate has a clear deliverable and deadline. The current position is marked with a pulsing indicator (in practice, a brightly colored marker).

### Design Elements to Steal

- **Spatial literature clustering**: Sources arranged by thematic area, with the gap at the intersection
- **Validity threat annotations**: Each methodology choice paired with threat and mitigation
- **Milestone-gated timeline**: Research phases connected by deliverable gates

## Example 4: Student Study Map — Organic Chemistry (The Learning Landscape)

### Board Summary

**Subject**: Organic Chemistry Fundamentals
**Level**: Undergraduate Chemistry
**Board Size**: 4200 x 3500px
**Palette**: K-8 Vibrant (adapted — higher saturation for engagement)

### What Makes It Exceptional

**1. The Central-Radial Layout Mirrors How Memory Works**

"Organic Chemistry" sits at the center. Three main branches radiate out:

- Carbon Bonding (left)
- Functional Groups (top-right)
- Nomenclature (bottom-right)

Each branch further subdivides into specific concepts, creating a tree that mirrors how the brain organizes related information. Students can literally "see" the knowledge structure.

**2. Mastery Checkpoints Are Self-Assessment Stations**

At the end of each branch, a mastery checkpoint poses a concrete question:

- Carbon Bonding: "Can you draw the orbital hybridization diagram for ethene and explain why C=C is a pi bond?"
- Functional Groups: "Given an unknown compound, can you identify its functional group from an IR spectrum?"
- Nomenclature: "Can you name this molecule using IUPAC rules: [complex structure drawn]?"

Students can self-assess by attempting the question before looking at the answer (revealed by zooming in on a nearby card).

**3. Cross-Links Reveal Hidden Connections**

Dashed lines cross between branches, showing non-obvious relationships:

- Functional groups → nomenclature: "The suffix tells you the functional group (-ol = alcohol, -al = aldehyde)"
- Carbon bonding → functional groups: "Hybridization determines bond geometry, which determines reactivity"

These cross-links transform isolated facts into an interconnected web.

### Design Elements to Steal

- **Central-radial knowledge architecture**: Core concept at center, detail radiating outward
- **Self-assessment mastery checkpoints**: Concrete questions at branch endpoints
- **Cross-branch connection lines**: Dashed lines showing relationships between different subtopics

## Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Shopping List Lesson Plan

**What it looks like**: "1. Do warmup. 2. Read pages 42-48. 3. Discuss. 4. Worksheet. 5. Homework."

**Why it fails**: There is no indication of WHY each activity exists, HOW it connects to objectives, WHAT cognitive level it targets, or HOW to differentiate. A substitute teacher could not teach this lesson. An observer could not evaluate its quality.

**The fix**: Add objectives with Bloom's verbs, align each activity to an objective, include timing, add teacher notes, and provide differentiation options.

### Anti-Pattern 2: The Objectives Graveyard

**What it looks like**: A lesson plan where objectives are listed in a corner but never referenced again. Activities proceed without any visible connection to the stated objectives.

**Why it fails**: The objectives are decoration, not design elements. They exist for compliance but do not drive the lesson.

**The fix**: Use connector lines, color coding, or spatial proximity to visually link every activity to its corresponding objective. Every objective should be referenced at least twice: once in an activity and once in an assessment.

### Anti-Pattern 3: The Bloom's Desert

**What it looks like**: A lesson where every activity targets "Remember" or "Understand." The entire 50 minutes is spent on listening, reading, and recalling facts.

**Why it fails**: Students never practice higher-order thinking. They memorize without understanding, recall without applying, and list without analyzing.

**The fix**: Ensure activities progress up Bloom's taxonomy. The first 30% of a lesson can target Remember/Understand, but the remaining 70% should include Apply, Analyze, and ideally Evaluate or Create.

### Anti-Pattern 4: The Invisible Differentiation

**What it looks like**: "Differentiation: Provide accommodations as needed."

**Why it fails**: This is not differentiation — it is a placeholder that signals no actual planning for diverse learners.

**The fix**: For each major activity, provide specific scaffold (simplified version), core (standard version), and extension (advanced version) instructions.

### Anti-Pattern 5: The Assessment Surprise

**What it looks like**: A lesson plan with detailed activities but an assessment that tests something not covered in the lesson — or no assessment at all.

**Why it fails**: Without aligned assessment, there is no way to know whether learning occurred. An unaligned assessment frustrates students and misrepresents their knowledge.

**The fix**: Design assessment BEFORE activities (backward design). Every assessment item should measure a specific objective that was explicitly taught.

## Before / After Transformations

### Before: Generic Lesson Plan

```
Topic: Photosynthesis
Objective: Understand photosynthesis
Activities: Lecture, lab, worksheet
Assessment: Quiz on Friday
```

### After: Aligned Lesson Plan

```
LO1 (Understand): Describe photosynthesis including inputs and outputs [5 min DI + Elodea demo]
LO2 (Apply): Label leaf cross-section structures and explain roles [10 min pair activity + word bank scaffold]
LO3 (Analyze): Predict glucose production changes if inputs are removed [10 min discussion + individual prediction]

Assessment: Exit ticket Q1→LO1, Q2→LO2, Q3→LO3
Differentiation: Word bank (scaffold) | Textbook reference (core) | No aids + energy flow arrows (extension)
```

### Before: Flat Course Curriculum

```
Week 1: Introduction
Week 2: Chapter 2
Week 3: Chapter 3
...
Week 15: Final Exam
```

### After: Designed Course Curriculum

```
Week 1: Digital Landscape [REMEMBER] — Ch. 1-2, no assignment
Week 2: Customer Data [UNDERSTAND] — Ch. 3 + HBR article, HW1: Channel audit (feeds into final project)
Week 3: Content Strategy [APPLY] — Ch. 4-5, HW2: Content plan for client project
Week 4: Social Media [ANALYZE] — Ch. 6 + Case study, Quiz 1 covering Wks 1-3
...
Week 14: Client Presentations [CREATE] — Final project presentations to industry panel
```
