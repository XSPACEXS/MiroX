# Business Model Canvas

## Overview

- **Purpose**: Map out a complete business model using Alexander Osterwalder's 9-block Business Model Canvas framework. Each block represents a core component of how a business creates, delivers, and captures value. The canvas provides a shared language for discussing business model assumptions, testing hypotheses, and communicating the model to investors, partners, and team members.
- **Best For**: Startup founders validating business ideas, product managers exploring new product lines, business strategists designing go-to-market approaches, MBA students analyzing case studies, consultants mapping client business models for transformation projects
- **Complexity**: Medium
- **Board Size**: 5000x3000px (wide landscape to accommodate all 9 blocks in the standard canvas layout)

## Example Content

The canvas maps "CloudSync Pro," a B2B project management SaaS platform at Series B stage. All financial figures, metrics, and market positions are realistic and internally consistent across all MiRoX Brain templates.

**Business model summary**: CloudSync Pro is a product-led growth SaaS company that combines project management, real-time collaboration, and AI automation in a single workspace. It monetizes through tiered subscriptions, with a land-and-expand strategy targeting SMB teams and upselling to mid-market and enterprise.

**Key hypotheses to test**:

1. "AI features will increase willingness to pay by 20% in mid-market segment"
2. "Salesforce AppExchange partnership will reduce CAC for mid-market by 30%"
3. "Enterprise segment will grow from 5% to 15% of customers within 18 months"

## Variations

1. **Lean Canvas (for startups)**: Replace Key Partners with "Problem," Key Activities with "Solution," Key Resources with "Unfair Advantage," and Customer Relationships with "Key Metrics." This is more suitable for early-stage startups testing problem-solution fit.

2. **Social Enterprise Canvas**: Add an "Impact" block in the center alongside Value Propositions. Replace Revenue Streams with "Surplus" and add a "Social/Environmental Cost" block alongside Cost Structure.

3. **Platform Canvas (two-sided)**: Duplicate Customer Segments, Value Props, Channels, and Relationships for two sides (e.g., buyers and sellers, or users and advertisers). The platform's value prop is connecting both sides.

4. **Innovation Canvas**: Focus on a single new feature or product within an existing business. Blocks become: Problem, Solution, Key Metrics, Unique Value Prop, Channels, Unfair Advantage, Customer Segments, Cost to Build, Revenue Expected.

## Build Instructions

1. **Create canvas**: 5000x3000px, background #F5F5F0.
2. **Draw the canvas grid**: Create the 9-block layout using frames with colored top borders. The grid should resemble the standard Osterwalder layout with the characteristic split on left (Partners full-height, Activities/Resources stacked) and right (Relationships/Channels stacked, Segments full-height).
3. **Add block headers**: Each block gets a bold header in the block's accent color, plus a guiding question in light italic.
4. **Populate stickies**: Add Soft Yellow sticky notes to each block with the specified content. Arrange in a single column within each block.
5. **Add segment cards**: In Customer Segments, create structured cards with persona details instead of simple stickies.
6. **Build cost and revenue visualizations**: In the bottom blocks, add horizontal bar representations of the breakdowns and key metric callouts.
7. **Draw connections**: Add dashed lines between related items across blocks (cost drivers, value chain connections, cross-block relationships).
8. **Add type labels**: Place small colored pills on stickies to classify items (partnership types, resource types, channel phases, etc.).
9. **Review**: Verify the value proposition addresses customer needs. Confirm cost structure supports the activities. Check that revenue streams match customer segments. Ensure the model is internally coherent.

## Tips & Best Practices

- **Start with Customer Segments and Value Propositions**: These are the core. Everything else exists to serve the customer-value connection. If these two blocks don't align, the model doesn't work.
- **One sticky per assumption**: Each sticky note represents a hypothesis about the business. Be specific enough that you could design an experiment to test it.
- **Use the canvas for conversations, not documentation**: The BMC is most valuable when discussed, debated, and iterated. Print it large and put it on a wall.
- **Update monthly for startups, quarterly for established businesses**: Business models are living things. Revisit assumptions regularly.
- **Color consistency**: Keep the Osterwalder color conventions (similar blocks share similar tints) so anyone familiar with the framework can read the board instantly.
- **Don't fill every block equally**: It's okay for Key Partners to have 3 items and Value Propositions to have 8. The imbalance reveals where the business model's complexity (and risk) concentrates.
- **Cross-reference with financials**: Every item in Cost Structure should trace to a specific Key Activity or Key Resource. Every Revenue Stream should trace to a specific Customer Segment. Orphaned items signal model gaps.
