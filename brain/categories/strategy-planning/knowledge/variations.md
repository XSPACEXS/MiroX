# Strategy & Planning — Variations

## Overview

Strategy boards must adapt to vastly different organizational contexts — a venture-backed startup's competitive analysis looks nothing like a non-profit's strategic assessment, yet both require the same analytical rigor and visual clarity. This document covers how to adapt strategy boards across industries, team sizes, complexity levels, cultural contexts, and integrations with other board types.

---

## Industry Adaptations

### SaaS / Software

**Framework emphasis**: SWOT + Competitive Positioning Map + OKR Planning
**Unique strategic concerns**: Product-market fit, churn analysis, unit economics, competitive moat, platform risk

**Content adaptations**:

- SWOT items reference SaaS-specific metrics: MRR, ARR, NRR, CAC, LTV, churn rate
- Competitive analysis includes pricing per seat, feature parity matrices, and API capability comparison
- OKRs cascade from product metrics (activation, retention, expansion) rather than generic growth goals
- Business Model Canvas emphasizes network effects, switching costs, and platform dynamics

**Visual adaptations**:

- Use the Strategic Clarity palette (Midnight Blue + Steel Gray) for a modern tech aesthetic
- Include a "Tech Stack Risk" zone in the SWOT threats quadrant for platform dependencies
- Competitive positioning maps often use "Feature Depth" vs. "Price" or "Ease of Use" vs. "Enterprise Readiness" axes
- Dashboard widgets showing MRR trends, churn rates, and NPS over time alongside the strategy framework

**Example SWOT item**: "NRR 115% (industry top quartile: 110%) — strong expansion revenue from existing customers reduces dependency on new logo acquisition"

### E-Commerce / Retail

**Framework emphasis**: SWOT + BMC + Vision-to-Execution Bridge
**Unique strategic concerns**: Seasonality, supply chain, customer acquisition cost, brand perception, omnichannel strategy

**Content adaptations**:

- SWOT items reference seasonal dynamics: "Q4 represents 45% of annual revenue — single-point-of-failure risk"
- BMC customer segments define by purchase behavior (frequency, AOV, channel preference) not just demographics
- Competitive analysis includes marketplace positioning (Amazon, Shopify) alongside direct competitors
- Vision boards integrate brand evolution with channel strategy

**Visual adaptations**:

- Use Competitive Fire palette for aggressive retail environments, or Balanced Wisdom for premium/luxury brands
- Include seasonal timeline overlay on Vision-to-Execution boards showing holiday preparation milestones
- Competitive positioning maps use "Price Point" vs. "Brand Perception" or "Selection Breadth" vs. "Curation Depth"
- Inventory and supply chain dependencies appear in the BMC Key Resources and Key Partners blocks

**Example SWOT item**: "Average order value $78 vs. competitor $52 — indicates stronger brand positioning but limits addressable market. 60% of orders include at least one bundled product."

### Healthcare / Biotech

**Framework emphasis**: SWOT + BMC + Risk Matrix (specialized four-quadrant)
**Unique strategic concerns**: Regulatory approval timelines, patient safety, clinical evidence, reimbursement pathways, compliance burden

**Content adaptations**:

- Every strategic item must consider regulatory implications (FDA, EMA, HIPAA, IRB)
- SWOT threats include regulatory risk as a permanent category — it never goes away
- BMC revenue streams detail reimbursement codes (CPT, ICD-10) and payer mix
- OKRs must balance business metrics with patient outcome metrics
- Competitive analysis includes clinical evidence levels (RCT, observational, case study) for each competitor's claims

**Visual adaptations**:

- Use Balanced Wisdom palette (earth tones convey trust and service orientation)
- Add a "Regulatory Status" indicator on every strategic item: Pre-submission, Under Review, Approved, Post-Market Surveillance
- Patient safety items always get the highest visual priority (red border, top position)
- BMC includes a specialized "Regulatory Pathway" block replacing or augmenting Key Activities
- Include a "Clinical Evidence" confidence system: RCT = solid border, Observational = dashed, Expert Opinion = dotted

**Example SWOT item**: "FDA 510(k) clearance achieved for Device v2.0 — 18-month head start over competitors still in pre-submission. However, post-market surveillance requirements increase operational cost by $200K/year."

### Financial Services / Fintech

**Framework emphasis**: SWOT + Competitive Positioning + Risk Matrix + BMC
**Unique strategic concerns**: Regulatory compliance (SEC, FINRA, PCI-DSS), trust, security posture, unit economics in thin-margin environments

**Content adaptations**:

- SWOT strengths emphasize security certifications, regulatory licenses, and trust indicators
- Competitive analysis includes regulatory approval status per jurisdiction
- BMC cost structure must account for compliance cost as a major line item
- OKRs balance growth with risk management — "grow deposits by 30%" must coexist with "maintain Tier 1 capital ratio above 12%"
- Threats always include cybersecurity, market volatility, and regulatory change

**Visual adaptations**:

- Use Executive Authority palette — financial stakeholders expect gravitas
- Include a "Risk Register" zone on SWOT boards showing probability x impact scoring
- Competitive positioning maps use "Trust/Brand" vs. "Technology/Innovation" axes
- Compliance status badges on every strategic item touching customer data or transactions
- Conservative typography — no experimental fonts, no playful elements

**Example SWOT item**: "PCI-DSS Level 1 certification + SOC2 Type II — strongest security posture in neobank category. Zero data breaches in 4 years of operation. Security is our primary acquisition driver — cited by 67% of enterprise prospects."

### Education / Non-Profit

**Framework emphasis**: SWOT + Vision Board + OKR Planning
**Unique strategic concerns**: Mission alignment, funding sources, impact measurement, stakeholder complexity (students, parents, donors, regulators, community)

**Content adaptations**:

- Replace revenue-focused metrics with impact metrics: students served, outcomes improved, access expanded
- SWOT strengths reference program efficacy data, not market share
- BMC revenue streams become "Funding Streams": grants, donations, government funding, earned revenue
- OKRs must satisfy multiple stakeholder groups simultaneously — donor expectations, student outcomes, community needs
- Competitive analysis reframes as "Landscape Analysis" — organizations in the same space are often collaborators, not competitors

**Visual adaptations**:

- Use Balanced Wisdom palette — warm, approachable, mission-driven
- Replace "Revenue" labels with "Impact" or "Funding" throughout
- Vision boards emphasize beneficiary stories alongside data
- Include a "Theory of Change" visual showing the causal chain from activities to outcomes
- Grant-specific callouts: "This strategy directly addresses [Foundation]'s priority area: [specific area]"

**Example SWOT item**: "Program alumni show 2.4x higher college enrollment rate vs. control group (n=1,847, longitudinal study 2019-2025). This is our strongest evidence for impact and our primary fundraising differentiator."

### Agency / Professional Services

**Framework emphasis**: BMC + Competitive Positioning + Vision-to-Execution Bridge
**Unique strategic concerns**: Utilization rate, talent retention, client concentration risk, service productization, recurring revenue transition

**Content adaptations**:

- BMC customer relationships emphasize the balance between customization (high margin, low scale) and productization (lower margin, high scale)
- SWOT weaknesses often include "key person risk" — if a senior partner leaves, clients may follow
- Competitive analysis focuses on positioning (generalist vs. specialist, local vs. global, boutique vs. full-service)
- OKRs balance financial health with knowledge development and team growth

**Visual adaptations**:

- Use Strategic Clarity palette — modern, professional, credible
- Include "Client Concentration" visualization: pie chart showing revenue by client (if top 3 clients > 40%, it is a strategic risk)
- Competitive positioning maps use "Specialization" vs. "Scale" or "Strategic Value" vs. "Execution Capability" axes
- BMC includes a "Talent" block (replacing or augmenting Key Resources) showing skill distribution and capacity

**Example SWOT item**: "Top 3 clients represent 52% of revenue — client concentration risk. If Client A (24% of revenue) churns, we have 4 months of runway. Mitigation: diversification target is no single client > 15% by Q4 2027."

## Team Size Adaptations

### Small Team (3-10 people)

**Board simplification**:

- Use a single framework per board (SWOT only, not SWOT + BMC + OKR combined)
- Reduce item count: 3-5 per SWOT quadrant, 2-3 per BMC block
- Skip the implications zone if the team is small enough to discuss implications verbally
- Board size: 4000-5000px per side

**Unique to small teams**:

- Every team member should see themselves in the strategy — at this size, everyone is a strategic actor
- OKRs can go directly from company to individual (skip department level)
- Competitive analysis can be simpler — 2-3 competitors instead of 6
- The board should be updatable in a single 30-minute team meeting

### Mid-Size Company (50-200 people)

**Standard setup**:

- Full framework boards with all sections populated
- OKR cascade: Company -> Department -> Team (3 levels)
- Competitive analysis: 4-6 competitors with detailed profiles
- Board size: 6000-8000px per side

**Unique to mid-size**:

- Cross-department alignment becomes critical — show how Engineering OKRs connect to Sales OKRs
- Multiple strategy boards may be needed: one company-level SWOT, one per-department OKR
- Include a "Strategic Alignment Check" showing which departments contribute to which strategic priorities
- The board needs to work without the CEO explaining it — distributed teams need self-documenting strategy

### Large Enterprise (500+ people)

**Enhanced structure**:

- Portfolio of strategy boards linked through a master strategic dashboard
- Company-level SWOT feeds into divisional strategies
- OKR cascade: Company -> Division -> Department -> Team (4 levels)
- Competitive analysis may be per-product-line, not just company-level
- Board size: 10000-14000px per side, or multiple linked boards

**Unique to enterprise**:

- Governance layer: who approves strategy changes, how often is the board updated, what is the review cadence
- Strategic consistency checking: do divisional strategies align with corporate strategy?
- Resource allocation: show how strategic priorities map to budget allocation
- Change management: strategy shifts require communication plans that the board should outline
- Board versioning: v1.0 (January Board), v1.1 (March Update), v2.0 (Mid-Year Revision)

## Complexity Levels

### Quick / Simple (20 minutes to build)

**Specifications**:

- Board size: 4000x4000px
- Single framework (SWOT or simple OKR)
- 3-5 items per section
- No cross-section connections
- No implications zone (verbal discussion instead)
- Default palette, minimal styling

**When to use**: Team offsite brainstorm, quick quarterly check-in, personal strategic thinking, project risk assessment

### Standard (45-60 minutes to build)

**Specifications**:

- Board size: 6000-8000px per side
- Full framework with all sections populated
- 4-8 items per section, evidence-backed
- 3-5 cross-section connections
- Implications zone with 3 priorities
- Custom palette, styled typography, confidence borders

**When to use**: Quarterly strategy review, board meeting preparation, investor update, team alignment session

### Advanced (2+ hours to build)

**Specifications**:

- Board size: 8000-14000px
- Multiple frameworks combined (SWOT + Competitive Map + OKR cascade)
- Comprehensive evidence with source citations
- Extensive cross-section and cross-framework connections
- Key Insight callout, implications zone, action plans with owners
- Full visual polish: palette, typography, scoring indicators, temporal markers
- Export-ready quality for external stakeholders

**When to use**: Annual strategic planning, major pivot assessment, M&A evaluation, fundraising preparation, board of directors presentation

## Cultural Considerations

### Distributed / Remote Organizations

- The board must be entirely self-documenting — no "I'll explain this in the meeting" assumptions
- Include a "How to Read This Board" frame in the top-left corner explaining the framework, color coding, and priority system
- Use explicit evidence labels — remote team members cannot ask clarifying questions in real-time
- Add "Last Updated" timestamps on individual items, not just the board header
- Include timezone-aware deadlines on OKR items and implications

### Conservative / Risk-Averse Cultures (Government, Insurance, Banking)

- Use muted, authoritative palettes (Executive Authority or Balanced Wisdom)
- Emphasize the Risk/Threat analysis — these organizations prioritize risk management over opportunity capture
- Include regulatory compliance checkpoints on every strategic initiative
- Use conservative language — "Evaluate" instead of "Disrupt," "Phased approach" instead of "Move fast"
- Confidence borders are especially important — distinguish confirmed facts from estimates rigorously

### Startup / Innovation Cultures

- Use bolder palettes (Competitive Fire or Strategic Clarity)
- Emphasize Opportunities over Threats — startup culture values opportunity capture
- Include speed metrics: "Time to market," "Sprint velocity," "Weeks to MVP"
- OKRs should be ambitious (0.7 target means you should be reaching for 1.0)
- Vision boards should be aspirational and even audacious — "10x not 10%"
- Competitive positioning maps should show market gaps as the primary focus

### Japanese / Lean Strategy Context

- Respect the Hoshin Kanri tradition — strategy deployment cascading from top to bottom and catching-ball back up
- Include Gemba (go and see) references — strategy should be grounded in direct observation
- Emphasize consensus-building indicators — show which strategic items have been discussed and agreed upon vs. proposed
- Include PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act) cycle markers on OKR initiatives
- Visual simplicity is valued — clean lines, minimal decoration, precise information

### European / Multi-Stakeholder Context

- Strategy boards may need to address multiple stakeholder groups: shareholders, employees, regulators, community, environment
- Include ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) considerations in the SWOT and BMC
- GDPR awareness: avoid displaying personal data; use role names instead of individual names on OKRs
- Multi-language considerations: if the board serves teams across countries, use clear terminology and avoid idioms

## Integration with Other Board Types

### Strategy Board to Mind Map

**Transition point**: When a strategic item needs deeper exploration — brainstorming solutions to a weakness, ideating on an opportunity, or decomposing a complex threat.

**How to connect**: Export the strategic item as the mind map's central topic. Use SWOT category as the framing: "How do we address our 12-day onboarding weakness?" The mind map explores solutions; the best ideas feed back into the strategy board's implications zone.

**Mapping**:

- SWOT item becomes mind map central topic
- Possible responses become mind map branches (tactical, strategic, resource-based, partnership-based)
- Best ideas become implications/actions on the strategy board

### Strategy Board to Project Management Board

**Transition point**: When strategic initiatives need execution tracking — an OKR initiative becomes a project with tasks.

**How to connect**: Each strategic initiative spawns a project on the PM board. OKR key results become project success metrics. The PM board references the originating OKR for alignment tracking.

**Mapping**:

- OKR Initiative becomes a project or epic
- Key Result becomes the project success metric
- Timeline from strategy board becomes project milestones
- Owner from implications becomes project lead

### Strategy Board to Marketing Board

**Transition point**: When strategic positioning needs to translate into marketing campaigns and messaging.

**How to connect**: Competitive positioning insights inform campaign messaging. SWOT strengths become value proposition messaging. Market opportunities become campaign targets.

**Mapping**:

- Competitive advantages (SWOT Strengths) become campaign messaging pillars
- Market opportunities become target audience definitions
- Competitive gaps become differentiation messaging
- Strategic timeline informs campaign calendar

### Strategy Board to Financial Model

**Transition point**: When strategic plans need financial validation — will this strategy lead to sustainable growth?

**How to connect**: BMC revenue and cost blocks feed into financial projections. OKR targets become financial targets. Strategic initiatives have associated budgets.

**Mapping**:

- BMC Revenue Streams become revenue model inputs
- BMC Cost Structure becomes expense model
- OKR metrics become financial KPIs
- Strategic initiative costs become budget line items

## Cross-Reference Quick Guide

| Starting Board  | Target Board    | Trigger                                           | What Transfers                               |
| --------------- | --------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| SWOT Analysis   | Mind Map        | Need to brainstorm responses to a weakness/threat | Strategic item becomes central topic         |
| SWOT Analysis   | Competitive Map | Need to visualize competitive position            | Strength/weakness data populates positioning |
| BMC             | Financial Model | Need to validate business model economics         | Revenue, cost, and unit economics data       |
| OKR Planning    | Sprint Board    | Initiative needs execution tracking               | Initiative becomes epic/project              |
| OKR Planning    | Dashboard       | Need to track OKR progress                        | Key Results become dashboard metrics         |
| Competitive Map | Campaign Board  | Need to message against competitors               | Positioning gaps become campaign angles      |
| Vision Board    | OKR Planning    | Need to operationalize the vision                 | Vision pillars become objectives             |

| SWOT            | Risk Register   | Need detailed risk management                     | Threats become risk register entries         |
| BMC             | Pitch Deck      | Need to present to investors                      | BMC blocks become pitch deck slides          |

## Framework Selection by Scenario

When users request a strategy board, use this decision tree to select the appropriate framework and variation:

```
What is the strategic context?

├── "We need to understand our current position"
│   ├── Small team, quick session → Simple SWOT (20 min)
│   ├── Board meeting or investor update → Full SWOT with evidence (60 min)
│   └── Comprehensive assessment → SWOT + Competitive Map (2+ hours)
│
├── "We need to set goals and align the team"
│   ├── Startup, 1 team → Simple OKR, 2 levels (30 min)
│   ├── Mid-size, multiple teams → Full OKR cascade, 3 levels (60 min)
│   └── Enterprise → OKR portfolio with cross-department links (2+ hours)
│
├── "We need to understand our business model"
│   ├── New venture or pivot → Lean Canvas variation (30 min)
│   ├── Established business → Full BMC with unit economics (60 min)
│   └── Business model transformation → BMC current + BMC future (2+ hours)
│
├── "We need to understand the competitive landscape"
│   ├── Quick positioning check → Simple 2-axis positioning map (20 min)
│   ├── Feature comparison → Positioning map + feature matrix (60 min)
│   └── Full competitive intelligence → Positioning + profiles + pricing (2+ hours)
│
└── "We need to translate vision to action"
    ├── Team alignment → Simple Vision-to-Execution, 3 columns (30 min)
    ├── Quarterly planning → Full 5-column bridge with metrics (60 min)
    └── Annual strategic plan → Vision bridge + OKR cascade + timeline (2+ hours)
```
