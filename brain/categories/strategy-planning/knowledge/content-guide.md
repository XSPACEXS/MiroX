# Strategy & Planning — Content Guide

## Overview

Strategy board content must be evidence-based, specific, and analytically rigorous. Generic strategic observations ("growing market," "strong brand") are worthless — they could apply to any company. God-level strategy boards contain insights that are uniquely relevant, data-backed, and actionable.

---

## Essential Sections by Framework

### SWOT Analysis

**Strengths** (Internal, Positive):

- Competitive advantages that are defensible and measurable
- Core competencies the team excels at relative to competitors
- Unique resources (patents, talent, technology, relationships)
- Example: "3 granted patents in NLP processing — 18-month lead over nearest competitor"
- Example: "Net Promoter Score of 72 (industry average: 34) — strongest in mid-market"
- NOT: "Good team" or "Strong brand" (too vague)

**Weaknesses** (Internal, Negative):

- Honest assessment of gaps, limitations, and disadvantages
- Areas where competitors outperform you
- Resource constraints or capability gaps
- Example: "No mobile app — 40% of competitor users access via mobile daily"
- Example: "Engineering team is 60% contractors — knowledge transfer risk on every project"
- NOT: "Need to improve marketing" (not specific enough)

**Opportunities** (External, Positive):

- Market trends, regulatory changes, or technology shifts you can exploit
- Unserved customer segments or needs
- Competitor weaknesses you can target
- Example: "EU Digital Services Act creates compliance burden that SMBs cannot handle alone — our automated compliance tool addresses this directly"
- Example: "Competitor X raised prices 40% — their NPS dropped to 21. 2,300 of their customers match our ICP."

**Threats** (External, Negative):

- Competitive moves that endanger your position
- Market shifts that undermine your model
- Regulatory, economic, or technological risks
- Example: "OpenAI launching competing product Q3 2026 — estimated 10x our engineering budget"
- Example: "Key supplier (AWS) raised API pricing 25% — directly impacts our margin from 67% to 52%"

### Business Model Canvas

**Customer Segments**: WHO are your customers?

- Define 2-4 distinct segments with specific criteria
- Example: "Enterprise SaaS (500+ employees, $10M+ ARR) — 60% of revenue"
- Example: "Mid-market startups (50-200 employees, Series A-B) — 30% of revenue, fastest growing"

**Value Propositions**: WHY do customers choose you?

- Specific benefits that map to specific customer pain points
- Example: "Reduces compliance audit preparation from 6 weeks to 3 days — saves $50K+ per audit"

**Channels**: HOW do you reach customers?

- Sales channels, marketing channels, distribution channels
- Example: "Inbound content marketing (blog + SEO) drives 45% of qualified leads at $28 CAC"

**Customer Relationships**: How do you KEEP customers?

- Relationship type per segment (dedicated account manager, self-service, community)
- Example: "Enterprise: Dedicated CSM assigned at onboarding. Quarterly business reviews. 96% retention."

**Revenue Streams**: How do you MAKE money?

- Pricing model, revenue per segment, growth trajectory
- Example: "SaaS subscriptions: $99/mo (starter), $499/mo (pro), custom enterprise. 85% MRR from Pro+Enterprise."

**Key Resources**: What do you NEED to deliver?

- People, technology, intellectual property, data, partnerships
- Example: "Proprietary ML model trained on 2.3M compliance documents — 94% accuracy, 3-year training head start"

**Key Activities**: What do you DO that matters most?

- Core operational activities that create and deliver value
- Example: "Continuous model retraining on new regulatory changes — weekly update cycle"

**Key Partners**: Who do you DEPEND on?

- Strategic partnerships, suppliers, platform providers
- Example: "AWS (infrastructure), Stripe (payments), Okta (SSO) — single points of dependency"

**Cost Structure**: What does it COST to operate?

- Fixed costs, variable costs, cost drivers
- Example: "AWS infrastructure: $45K/mo (variable). Team: $380K/mo (fixed). Total burn: $520K/mo."

### OKR Planning

**Company-Level Objectives** (3-5 per quarter):

- Ambitious, qualitative goals that inspire
- Example: "Become the undisputed leader in automated compliance for mid-market SaaS"

**Key Results per Objective** (2-4 per objective):

- Measurable, time-bound outcomes
- Example: "Increase MRR from $420K to $650K by end of Q2"
- Example: "Achieve 50+ enterprise customer logo count (currently: 31)"
- Example: "Reduce customer onboarding time from 14 days to 3 days"

**Team-Level OKRs** (aligned to company OKRs):

- Show explicit connection: "Supports Company O2-KR1"
- Example: "Engineering O1: Ship API v3.0 with GraphQL support — Supports Company O1"

**Individual OKRs** (optional, aligned to team):

- Personal contributions that ladder up
- Score tracking: 0.0 to 1.0 scale (0.7 = target, 1.0 = stretch)

### Competitive Analysis

**Competitor Profiles** (3-6 competitors):

- Company name, founding year, funding, revenue (if known)
- Target market and positioning statement
- Key product features with comparison to ours
- Pricing (exact numbers)
- Recent strategic moves

**Comparison Matrix**:

| Feature               | Us              | Competitor A | Competitor B | Competitor C        |
| --------------------- | --------------- | ------------ | ------------ | ------------------- |
| AI-powered analysis   | Full            | Partial      | None         | Full                |
| Enterprise SSO        | Yes             | Yes          | No           | Yes                 |
| Mobile app            | No              | Yes          | Yes          | No                  |
| Pricing (per seat/mo) | $49             | $79          | $29          | $99                 |
| API access            | Full            | Limited      | None         | Full                |
| Compliance coverage   | SOC2+GDPR+HIPAA | SOC2 only    | SOC2+GDPR    | SOC2+GDPR+HIPAA+PCI |

**Positioning Map Data**: X-axis and Y-axis definitions with competitor placement coordinates

### Vision Board

**Mission Statement**: Why do we exist? (1-2 sentences)
**Vision Statement**: Where are we going? (1-2 sentences, aspirational future state)
**Core Values**: 3-5 non-negotiable principles (with behavioral definitions)
**Strategic Pillars**: 3-5 major themes that guide all decisions
**Long-Term Goals**: 3-5 year aspirational targets with metrics

## Writing Guidelines for Strategy Content

### The Specificity Test

Every strategic item should pass this test: "Could a competitor put this exact same statement on their board?" If yes, it is too generic. Rewrite until the answer is no.

- Generic: "We have a strong engineering team" (every company says this)
- Specific: "Our ML team includes 3 PhDs from Stanford's NLP lab, giving us a 2-year research advantage in document classification accuracy"

### The Evidence Test

Every strategic item should include or reference supporting evidence:

- Market data with source and date
- Internal metrics with current values
- Customer quotes or survey data with sample size
- Competitive intelligence with verification source

### The "So What?" Test

Every item on the board should answer "So what?" — what does this mean for our strategy?

- Fact: "Competitor X raised $50M Series C"
- So what: "They will likely expand into our mid-market segment within 12 months. We need to lock in enterprise contracts with multi-year commitments before they arrive."

### Writing Style for Strategy Items

**Headline format**: "Metric + Context + Implication"

- "NPS 72 (industry avg 34) — our strongest competitive moat in mid-market retention"
- "40% mobile usage gap — losing enterprise evaluations where mobile demo is required"
- "EU DSA deadline Q4 2026 — 18-month window to establish automated compliance category leadership"

## Content Volume Guidelines

| Framework    | Items per Section                          | Total Items       | Max Depth                       |
| ------------ | ------------------------------------------ | ----------------- | ------------------------------- |
| SWOT         | 4-8 per quadrant                           | 16-32 total       | 2 levels (item + detail)        |
| BMC          | 3-6 per block                              | 27-54 total       | 1 level (items only)            |
| OKR          | 3-5 objectives, 2-4 KRs each               | 15-25 total       | 3 levels (O, KR, Initiative)    |
| Competitive  | 3-6 competitors, 5-8 comparison dimensions | 15-48 data points | 2 levels (profile + comparison) |
| Vision Board | 1-5 per section                            | 15-25 total       | 1 level                         |

### Warning Signs

**Too much content**: The framework's structure is lost in a sea of stickies. The viewer cannot identify the key insights. The board requires more than 5 minutes to process.

**Too little content**: Sections have 1-2 items. The analysis feels shallow. Critical dimensions of the framework are empty.

**Wrong content**: Items in the wrong section (an external factor in the Strengths quadrant). Content that is opinions rather than evidence. Historical facts without strategic implications.

## Questions the Board Should Answer

### For the CEO / Board

1. What is our strategic position? (30 seconds)
2. What are the 3 biggest opportunities? (1 minute)
3. What are the 3 biggest risks? (1 minute)
4. How does our strategy differentiate us from competitors? (2 minutes)
5. Where should we invest next? (3 minutes)

### For the Strategy Team

1. Are all critical factors captured? (5 minutes — completeness check)
2. What connections exist between strengths and opportunities? (5 minutes)
3. What data gaps exist in our analysis? (3 minutes)
4. What strategic options emerge from this analysis? (10 minutes)

### For Team Leaders

1. How does my team's work connect to the overall strategy? (2 minutes)
2. What are my team's specific OKRs? (1 minute)
3. What strategic risks should my team be aware of? (2 minutes)
